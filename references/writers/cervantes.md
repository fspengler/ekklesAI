# Miguel de Cervantes (米格尔·德·塞万提斯, 1547–1616)

## Life

Cervantes was born in Alcalá de Henares, Spain, into a family of modest means. His father was a barber-surgeon. Little is known of his education.

In 1571, he fought at the Battle of Lepanto against the Ottoman fleet, where he was wounded and lost the use of his left hand—he called it "the most glorious occasion past or present ages have witnessed." In 1575, he was captured by Barbary pirates and spent five years as a slave in Algiers before being ransomed.

He returned to Spain and struggled as a writer, tax collector, and purchasing agent. He was imprisoned at least twice for financial irregularities. In 1605, at age 57, he published Part One of Don Quixote. It was an immediate success. Part Two followed in 1615. He died in Madrid in 1616—the same year as Shakespeare.

## Writing Style

- **Metafiction**: Characters discuss their own story; Part Two characters have read Part One
- **Multiple perspectives**: Different narrators, found manuscripts, competing versions
- **Irony and ambiguity**: Is Quixote mad or wise? The novel never settles
- **Realism within fantasy**: The physical world is concrete even as Quixote transforms it
- **Dialogue as revelation**: Characters emerge through how they speak
- **Genre mixing**: Romance, picaresque, pastoral, all parodied and transcended

## Major Works

### Don Quixote (《堂吉诃德》, Part I 1605, Part II 1615)

**Theme**: Idealism vs. reality, madness and wisdom, the power of fiction, the nature of identity.

**Content**:

**Part One**:
Alonso Quixano, a hidalgo in La Mancha, has read so many chivalric romances that he loses his mind. He believes himself a knight-errant, renames himself Don Quixote de la Mancha, dubs a peasant girl Dulcinea del Toboso as his lady, and sets out to right wrongs.

He recruits Sancho Panza, a simple farmer, as his squire, promising him an island to govern. Together they ride out. Quixote sees giants where there are windmills, armies where there are sheep, castles where there are inns. He is beaten, mocked, and defeated—yet persists.

Interpolated stories interrupt the narrative. The novel plays with its own authorship (the "found manuscript" of Cide Hamete Benengeli).

**Part Two**:
Ten years later, Quixote and Sancho are famous—Part One has been published, and people have read it. Characters they meet have expectations based on the book. A false sequel by another author (Avellaneda) is denounced within the text.

A Duke and Duchess entertain Quixote and Sancho, staging elaborate pranks. Sancho finally gets his island (Barataria) and governs wisely before giving it up. Quixote descends into the Cave of Montesinos and has visions. He is finally defeated by the Knight of the White Moon (his neighbor in disguise) and returns home. On his deathbed, he recovers his sanity, renounces his chivalric fantasies, and dies as Alonso Quixano.

### Novelas Ejemplares (《训诫小说集》, 1613)

Twelve short novels (novellas) ranging from idealistic romance to picaresque realism:
- "Rinconete and Cortadillo" (picaresque)
- "The Jealous Old Man from Extremadura"
- "The Deceitful Marriage" and "The Dialogue of the Dogs" (a dog narrates)
- "The Glass Graduate" (a man believes he is made of glass)

---

### Los Trabajos de Persiles y Sigismunda (《贝尔西莱斯和西希斯蒙达历险记》, 1617)

A Byzantine romance, published posthumously. Cervantes considered it his masterpiece. Two lovers journey from the frozen north to Rome.

## Famous Lines

### On Reading
"Finally, from so little sleeping and so much reading, his brain dried up and he went completely out of his mind."

### The Windmills
"Look there, friend Sancho Panza, where thirty or more monstrous giants appear. I intend to do battle with them and slay them."
"What giants?" asked Sancho.
"Those you see over there," replied his master, "with the long arms."
"Those are not giants but windmills."

### On Dulcinea
"God knows whether there is a Dulcinea in the world or not, or whether she is fantastic or not fantastic; these are not matters whose verification can be carried out to the full."

### On Fiction and Reality
"For me alone Don Quixote was born, and I for him; he knew how to act, and I to write; we two are one."

### On Freedom
"Liberty, Sancho, is one of the most precious gifts heaven has bestowed upon men; no treasures that the earth contains or the sea conceals can be compared with it."

### On Death
"There are no birds this year in last year's nests."

### Sancho's Wisdom
"Whether the pitcher hits the stone or the stone hits the pitcher, it's bad for the pitcher."

### Final Words
"In last year's nests there are no birds this year. I was mad, now I am sane; I was Don Quixote de la Mancha, I am now Alonso Quixano the Good."

## Themes

### Illusion and Reality
Is Quixote mad or does he see truths others miss? The novel holds both possibilities. His "madness" transforms the world—and perhaps that transformation is what meaning is.

### The Power of Fiction
Characters discuss the novel they are in. Reading has made Quixote who he is. Fiction shapes reality.

### Idealism
Quixote's idealism is absurd—and noble. He believes in honor, love, justice when everyone around him is cynical. The laughter he provokes is tinged with admiration.

### Friendship
Quixote and Sancho—knight and squire, idealist and realist—complete each other. Their conversations are the heart of the novel.

### Identity
Who is the "real" person—Alonso Quixano or Don Quixote? Can we choose who we are?

## Influence

- **The novel**: Don Quixote is often called the first modern novel. Its techniques—unreliable narration, metafiction, psychological depth—define the form.
- **Quixotic**: The word itself means noble but impractical idealism
- **All subsequent fiction**: Fielding, Sterne, Dostoevsky, Flaubert, Borges, Nabokov—everyone responds to Cervantes
- **The figure of the knight**: Parodied and yet immortalized

## In Writing

When channeling Cervantes, you:
- Mix genres: romance, comedy, tragedy, picaresque
- Play with narrative frames and authorship
- Let idealism and realism collide without resolution
- Create dialogue that reveals character
- Find wisdom in apparent folly
- Remember: the knight who tilts at windmills may be the sanest of all
