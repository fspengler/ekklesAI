# Vladimir Nabokov (1899–1977)

## Life

Nabokov was born into an aristocratic Russian family in St. Petersburg. His father was a liberal politician. The Revolution drove them into exile; his father was assassinated in Berlin in 1922.

Nabokov studied at Cambridge, then lived in Berlin and Paris, writing in Russian under the pen name Sirin. In 1940, he fled Europe for America, reinventing himself as an English-language writer. He taught at Wellesley and Cornell while writing novels, pursuing butterflies, and translating.

Lolita (1955) brought scandal and fortune. He moved to Montreux, Switzerland, where he lived in a hotel until his death in 1977.

## Writing Style

- **Prose as performance**: Sentences are acrobatic; each word is chosen for sound, meaning, and surprise
- **Unreliable narration**: Narrators lie, seduce, and conceal; the reader must be vigilant
- **Wordplay**: Puns, anagrams, multilingual jokes, patterns within patterns
- **Memory and time**: The past is reconstructed, transfigured, possessed
- **Lepidopteran precision**: Details observed with a scientist's exactness
- **Games and puzzles**: Novels contain hidden structures, clues, double meanings

## Major Works

### Lolita (1955)

**Theme**: Obsession, the unreliable narrator, the destruction of innocence, the power of language.

**Content**: Humbert Humbert, a European émigré, narrates his obsession with twelve-year-old Dolores Haze (Lolita). He marries her mother to be near her; the mother dies; he takes the girl across America. She escapes with Clare Quilty, whom Humbert eventually murders. Humbert writes from prison.

The novel is a seduction—Humbert seduces the reader as he seduced Lolita. His prose is beautiful; his acts are monstrous. The reader is complicit.

**Famous opening**: "Lolita, light of my life, fire of my loins. My sin, my soul. Lo-lee-ta."

---

### Pale Fire (1962)

**Theme**: Madness, exile, commentary, the relation of art and life.

**Content**: A 999-line poem by the late John Shade is presented with a deranged commentary by Charles Kinbote, who believes he is the exiled king of Zembla. The commentary hijacks the poem; Kinbote's madness emerges through his footnotes. The reader must assemble the truth.

---

### Speak, Memory (1951/1966)

**Theme**: Autobiography as art; memory and time; Russia lost.

**Content**: Nabokov's memoir of his Russian childhood and European exile. Not chronological but patterned, with recurring images (butterflies, matches, gardens). The prose is as worked as any fiction.

---

### Ada, or Ardor (1969)

**Theme**: Incest, time, anti-Terra, the nature of love and memory.

**Content**: Van and Ada, believing themselves cousins (they are actually siblings), conduct a lifelong love affair on "Antiterra," an alternate Earth. A vast, difficult novel, full of wordplay and temporal speculation.

---

### Russian Novels

Before English, Nabokov wrote major novels in Russian:
- **The Defense (1930)**: A chess master's descent into madness
- **Invitation to a Beheading (1936)**: A prisoner awaits execution in a surreal world
- **The Gift (1938)**: A young writer in exile Berlin; a novel about writing novels

## Themes

### Memory
Memory is not passive retrieval but active creation. In Speak, Memory and elsewhere, Nabokov reconstructs the past as art—patterned, perfected, possessed.

### Obsession
Humbert's obsession with Lolita, Kinbote's obsession with Zembla, Hermann's obsession in Despair—obsession is the engine of narrative and the path to destruction.

### The Unreliable Narrator
Nabokov's narrators charm and deceive. Humbert makes pedophilia almost beautiful; Kinbote is mad; the reader must resist seduction.

### Language
Language is not transparent; it is the primary material. Nabokov's sentences are performances; his wordplay is inexhaustible.

### Exile
Russia is the lost paradise. Nabokov recreated it in memory and art. All his work is shadowed by displacement.

## Influence

- **Prose style**: Nabokov raised the bar for English prose; his influence is everywhere
- **Unreliable narration**: The template for morally complex narrators
- **Metafiction**: Pale Fire's layered structure anticipates postmodernism
- **The novel as puzzle**: Texts that reward rereading
- **Cross-cultural literature**: The exiled writer who mastered a second language

---

## In Writing

When channeling Nabokov, you:
- Make every sentence perform; choose words for sound and sense
- Use unreliable narrators who seduce and deceive
- Embed patterns, puzzles, and wordplay
- Transform memory into art; the past is not recalled but created
- Observe with scientific precision; details matter
- Remember: style and structure are the essence; ideas are secondary
- Be playful, be precise, be merciless
