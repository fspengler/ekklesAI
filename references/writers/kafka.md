# Franz Kafka (弗朗茨·卡夫卡, 1883–1924)

## Life

Kafka was born in Prague into a German-speaking Jewish family. His father Hermann was a domineering businessman whose disapproval haunted Franz forever. He studied law, worked at an insurance company, and wrote at night.

He was engaged three times—twice to Felice Bauer—but never married. He feared that marriage would destroy his writing. His relationships were conducted largely through letters.

He published little during his lifetime: a few stories, none of the novels complete. He asked his friend Max Brod to burn his manuscripts after his death. Brod disobeyed, and the novels were published posthumously.

Kafka died of tuberculosis in 1924 at age 40.

## Writing Style

- **Nightmarish logic**: Impossible situations described with bureaucratic precision
- **Clarity in absurdity**: Plain, exact prose that makes the impossible feel inevitable
- **The excluded protagonist**: Characters cannot enter, cannot understand, cannot belong
- **Allegory that resists interpretation**: The meanings multiply; no key unlocks everything
- **The father**: Authority, judgment, power—always overwhelming
- **Animals and transformation**: Insects, apes, dogs—becoming other

## Major Works

### The Metamorphosis (《变形记》, Die Verwandlung, 1915)

**Theme**: Alienation, family, the body as burden, being seen as monstrous.

**Content**: Gregor Samsa wakes one morning to find himself transformed into a giant insect. He cannot work; his family—whom he has supported—recoils. His sister Grete cares for him at first, then turns against him. He becomes a burden. When he dies, the family is relieved and goes on a hopeful outing.

**Famous opening**: "As Gregor Samsa awoke one morning from uneasy dreams, he found himself transformed in his bed into a monstrous vermin."

---

### The Trial (《审判》, Der Prozess, 1925, posthumous)

**Theme**: Guilt without crime; law without justice; the individual crushed by the system.

**Content**: Josef K. is arrested on his thirtieth birthday. He is never told the charge. He tries to defend himself, engages lawyers, seeks access to the court—but the system is impenetrable, absurd, everywhere. After a year, two men come for him and execute him "like a dog."

**Famous opening**: "Someone must have been telling lies about Josef K., for without having done anything wrong, he was arrested one fine morning."

---

### The Castle (《城堡》, Das Schloss, 1926, posthumous, unfinished)

**Theme**: The inaccessible authority; the outsider who cannot belong; the search for meaning.

**Content**: K. arrives in a village claiming to be a land surveyor summoned by the Castle. The Castle neither confirms nor denies. He struggles to gain access, to understand the bureaucracy, to establish himself. He never reaches the Castle. The novel breaks off.

---

### America (《美国》, Amerika / The Man Who Disappeared, 1927, posthumous)

**Theme**: The immigrant, the new world, innocence and exploitation.

**Content**: Karl Rossmann, sent to America by his family after a scandal, experiences a series of adventures—kindly uncles, exploitative friends, strange hotels, the Nature Theatre of Oklahoma. Lighter than the other novels, but still Kafkaesque.

### Short Stories

#### In the Penal Colony (1919)
An officer explains an execution machine that inscribes the condemned man's sentence on his body. The machine breaks down; the officer places himself in it.

#### A Hunger Artist (1922)
A professional faster exhibits himself in a cage. The public loses interest. He fasts to death, confessing he only fasted because he never found food he liked.

#### The Judgment (1913)
Georg Bendemann's father rises from his bed and condemns Georg to death by drowning. Georg carries out the sentence.

#### A Report to an Academy (1917)
An ape who has learned to be human delivers a lecture on his transformation.

---

### Letter to His Father (1919)

A 45-page letter (never sent) in which Kafka anatomizes his relationship with his father: the fear, the inadequacy, the impossibility of communication.

## Themes

### The Bureaucratic Nightmare
Courts, castles, offices—systems that are everywhere and nowhere, powerful and absurd. The individual cannot understand the rules, cannot reach the authorities, is always guilty.

### Alienation
From family, from society, from one's own body. Gregor becomes a bug; K. is an outsider; Josef K. is suddenly a criminal.

### The Father
Hermann Kafka looms behind the work. The father's judgment is absolute, incomprehensible, crushing. In "The Judgment," the father's sentence is death.

### Guilt
Kafka's characters are guilty but do not know their crime. Guilt is ontological—existing is already transgression.

### Transformation
Bodies change (Gregor, the ape). The world is unstable. One wakes into nightmare.

## Influence

- **Kafkaesque**: The word entered the language; the adjective became definition
- **Existentialism**: Camus, Sartre, Beckett—Kafka is their ancestor
- **Absurdist fiction**: The template for the twentieth century's anxieties
- **Magical realism**: The precise rendering of the impossible
- **Every dystopia**: Orwell, Huxley, and beyond owe debts to The Trial and The Castle

---

## In Writing

When channeling Kafka, you:
- Describe the impossible with bureaucratic precision
- Create systems that are both absurd and inescapable
- Make the protagonist excluded, seeking, never arriving
- Refuse to explain; let the allegory remain open
- Use the father as a figure of crushing authority
- Write clearly: the strangeness is in the situation, not the style
- Remember: there is infinite hope—but not for us
