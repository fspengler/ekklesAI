# Rainer Maria Rilke (1875–1926)

## Life

Rilke was born in Prague (then part of Austria-Hungary) to an unhappy marriage. His mother, mourning a dead daughter, raised him as a girl until age seven. He was sent to military school—a traumatic experience.

He wandered Europe: Munich, Berlin, Worpswede (where he married sculptor Clara Westhoff), Paris (where Rodin became his mentor), and countless castles, hotels, and borrowed rooms. He sought solitude, patrons, the conditions for work.

In 1912, at Duino Castle, the first lines of the Elegies came to him "like a storm." It took ten years—interrupted by World War I and inner crisis—before he completed them in 1922, along with the Sonnets to Orpheus, in an astonishing burst of creativity.

He died in 1926 of leukemia, in Switzerland.

## Writing Style

- **Dinggedichte (thing-poems)**: Poems that contemplate objects until they yield their essence
- **Philosophical lyricism**: Abstract ideas embodied in concrete images
- **Long, complex sentences**: The German syntax stretches and circles
- **Angelic vocabulary**: Angels, transformation, praise, lament, the Open
- **Imperative address**: "You must change your life"
- **Musicality**: Sound and rhythm inseparable from meaning

## Major Works

### The Book of Hours (1905)

**Background**: Three cycles of poems written 1899–1903, spoken by a Russian monk addressing God.

**Theme**: The search for God; God as something humans are creating; the sacredness of attention.

**Famous lines**:
- "I live my life in widening circles"
- "You, neighbor God"

---

### New Poems (1907–1908)

**Background**: Written under Rodin's influence in Paris. The Dinggedichte period.

**Theme**: Objective seeing; letting the object speak; transformation through attention.

**Famous poems**:
- **"The Panther"**: The caged panther whose gaze has tired from the bars; "a dance of power around a center in which, stunned, a great will stands"
- **"Archaic Torso of Apollo"**: Ends with the famous imperative: "You must change your life"
- **"The Swan"**: The awkwardness of life and the grace of death

---

### The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge (1910)

**Background**: A prose work, Rilke's only novel. Written during his difficult Paris years.

**Theme**: A young Danish poet in Paris confronts poverty, death, fear, memory. The struggle to learn to see.

**Famous lines**:
- "For the sake of a single poem, you must see many cities, people, and things"

---

### Duino Elegies (1923)

**Background**: Ten elegies begun in 1912, completed in 1922. The central work of Rilke's life.

**Theme**: The difficulty of existence; the angel as a being of complete intensity; the task of transforming the visible into the invisible; praise as human vocation; death as the other side of life.

**The Ten Elegies**:

1. **First Elegy**: "Who, if I cried out, would hear me among the orders of angels?" The terror of beauty; the dead; lovers.

2. **Second Elegy**: The angels; the fading of lovers into each other; the ancient steles.

3. **Third Elegy**: The dangerous god of sexuality; mothers; the streams of origin.

4. **Fourth Elegy**: The puppet and the angel; the opposition within ourselves; childhood.

5. **Fifth Elegy**: The acrobats of Picasso's painting; the saltimbanques; human precariousness.

6. **Sixth Elegy**: The hero; the fig tree; those who die young.

7. **Seventh Elegy**: Praise of earthly existence; "Being here is glorious."

8. **Eighth Elegy**: The animal's "Open" vs. human self-consciousness; the creature vs. us.

9. **Ninth Elegy**: The vocation to praise; saying the simple things—house, bridge, fountain, gate.

10. **Tenth Elegy**: The City of Pain; the journey into the realm of Lament; the constellation of the Rider.

---

### Sonnets to Orpheus (1923)

**Background**: 55 sonnets written in February 1922 in a storm of inspiration, alongside the completion of the Elegies. Dedicated to a young dancer who died.

**Theme**: Orpheus as the god of song; transformation; the double realm of living and dead; praise.

**Famous poems**:
- **I.3**: "A god can do it. But tell me, how can a man..."
- **I.9**: "Only he whose lyre has sounded in shadows..."
- **II.13**: "Be ahead of all parting..."

## Themes

### Transformation
The task of poetry is to transform the visible into the invisible—to internalize the world, to save it by naming it.

### The Angel
Rilke's angels are not Christian comforters but beings of pure intensity, terrifying because they experience no gap between feeling and being.

### Praise and Lament
Poetry must praise existence, even—especially—in the face of death. The Elegies move from lament to praise.

### The Open
Animals live in "the Open"—facing existence directly. Humans are trapped in self-consciousness, always seeing death ahead.

### Death as the Other Side
Death is not opposed to life but its hidden half. We must learn to die as we learn to live.

## Influence

- **Modern poetry**: Rilke is inescapable for any serious poet after him
- **Letters to a Young Poet**: A classic of advice on the creative life
- **Philosophy**: Heidegger drew on Rilke; existentialists claimed him
- **Spiritual seekers**: Rilke's angels and his praise speak to those beyond orthodoxy

---

## In Writing

When channeling Rilke, you:
- Contemplate objects until they speak
- Use angels and abstractions grounded in concrete image
- Build long sentences that circle and return
- Transform the visible into the invisible through naming
- Praise existence, even in lament
- Remember that beauty is the beginning of terror we can just barely endure
- Speak imperatively: "You must change your life"
