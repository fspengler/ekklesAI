# Homer (c. 8th century BCE)

## Life

Homer is more legend than biography. The ancient Greeks believed he was a blind poet from Ionia (perhaps Smyrna or Chios) who lived around the 8th century BCE. Seven cities claimed him as their native son. Whether Homer was one man, several poets, or a tradition refined over generations remains debated—the "Homeric Question."

What matters: two epics attributed to him became the foundation of Western literature, the textbooks of Greek education, and the source from which all epic poetry flows.

## Writing Style

- **Oral-formulaic composition**: Epithets (swift-footed Achilles, rosy-fingered Dawn), repeated phrases, and type-scenes enabled memorization and live performance
- **Hexameter**: The dactylic hexameter line, the rhythm of Greek epic
- **Similes**: Extended comparisons that pause the action to illuminate it (warriors like lions, armies like waves)
- **In medias res**: Beginning in the middle of the action
- **Divine machinery**: Gods intervene constantly, yet human choice remains central
- **Objectivity**: Homer rarely judges; he shows, presenting all sides with sympathy

## Complete Works

### Iliad

**Background**: Set in the tenth year of the Trojan War. Not the whole war but a few weeks focused on Achilles' wrath.

**Theme**: The wrath of Achilles and its consequences; honor, mortality, war, compassion.

**Content Summary**:

- **Books 1–4**: Agamemnon takes Achilles' prize Briseis; Achilles withdraws from battle. The Greeks suffer. Zeus promises Thetis he will honor Achilles by letting the Trojans win.

- **Books 5–8**: Battle scenes. Diomedes' aristeia (heroic excellence). Hector advances. The Greeks are pushed back to their ships.

- **Books 9–12**: Embassy to Achilles fails; he refuses gifts and refuses to fight. Patroclus grows concerned. The Trojans breach the Greek wall.

- **Books 13–17**: Fierce fighting. Patroclus begs to enter battle. Achilles lends him his armor. Patroclus drives back the Trojans but is killed by Hector.

- **Books 18–22**: Achilles' grief and rage. Thetis brings new armor from Hephaestus. Achilles returns, slaughters Trojans, kills Hector, drags his body behind his chariot.

- **Books 23–24**: Funeral games for Patroclus. Priam comes alone to Achilles' tent to ransom Hector's body. The two enemies weep together. Achilles returns the body. Hector's funeral.

**The arc**: From wrath to compassion. Achilles, consumed by rage, recovers his humanity when he sees in Priam his own father.

### Odyssey

**Background**: The ten-year journey of Odysseus returning home to Ithaca after the Trojan War.

**Theme**: Homecoming (nostos), cunning (metis), identity, endurance, civilization vs. savagery.

**Content Summary**:

- **Books 1–4 (Telemachia)**: Odysseus has been gone 20 years. Suitors plague his house, courting Penelope and consuming his wealth. His son Telemachus, guided by Athena, travels to seek news of his father.

- **Books 5–8**: Odysseus on Calypso's island; the gods order his release. He builds a raft, is shipwrecked by Poseidon, and washes ashore among the Phaeacians. King Alcinous hosts him.

- **Books 9–12 (Apologoi)**: Odysseus narrates his wanderings: the Lotus-Eaters, the Cyclops Polyphemus (whom he blinds, earning Poseidon's wrath), Aeolus and the bag of winds, the Laestrygonians, Circe, the Underworld (where he meets the dead), the Sirens, Scylla and Charybdis, the cattle of the Sun (which his men eat, dooming them).

- **Books 13–16**: The Phaeacians return Odysseus to Ithaca. Athena disguises him as a beggar. He meets his faithful swineherd Eumaeus. Telemachus returns; father and son reunite.

- **Books 17–20**: Odysseus enters his own hall as a beggar. The suitors abuse him. Penelope brings out Odysseus' bow for a contest.

- **Books 21–24**: Odysseus strings the bow, reveals himself, and slaughters the suitors with Telemachus. He is reunited with Penelope (who tests him with the secret of their bed). Peace is restored.

**The arc**: From nobody (Outis, the name he gives the Cyclops) to king restored. Odysseus must prove his identity through cunning, endurance, and knowledge that only he possesses.

## Homeric Techniques

### Epithets
Fixed phrases attached to characters:
- "Swift-footed Achilles" (podas ōkus Achilleus)
- "Rosy-fingered Dawn" (rhododaktylos Ēōs)
- "Wine-dark sea" (oinops pontos)
- "Resourceful Odysseus" (polymētis Odysseus)
- "Hector of the glinting helm"

### Extended Similes
Comparisons that expand into mini-scenes:
- Armies like swarms of bees
- A warrior like a lion among cattle
- Grief like snow melting on mountains

### Type-Scenes
Repeated patterns: arming for battle, sacrifices, feasts, arrivals, recognitions.

### Ring Composition
Narratives that circle back to where they began; themes echoed in reverse order.

## Famous Passages

### The Opening of the Iliad
"Sing, O goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus, that brought countless ills upon the Achaeans."

μῆνιν ἄειδε θεὰ Πηληϊάδεω Ἀχιλῆος
οὐλομένην, ἣ μυρί᾽ Ἀχαιοῖς ἄλγε᾽ ἔθηκε

### The Opening of the Odyssey
"Tell me, O Muse, of that man of many ways, who was driven far and wide after he sacked the sacred citadel of Troy."

ἄνδρα μοι ἔννεπε, μοῦσα, πολύτροπον, ὃς μάλα πολλὰ
πλάγχθη

### Achilles to Priam (Iliad 24)
"Such is the way the gods have spun the thread for wretched mortals—to live in pain, while they themselves are free from care. Two urns stand on the floor of Zeus's palace, filled with gifts he gives: one of evil, one of good."

### Glaucus on Mortality (Iliad 6)
"As is the generation of leaves, so is that of men. The wind scatters the leaves to the ground, but the forest blooms again when spring returns. So one generation of men is born as another passes away."

### Achilles on Glory and Death (Iliad 9)
"My mother Thetis tells me that two fates carry me toward death: if I stay here and fight around Troy, I lose my homecoming but my glory will be eternal; if I go home, I lose that glory but my life will be long."

### Odysseus Reveals Himself (Odyssey 9)
"I am Odysseus, son of Laertes, known to all men for my cunning, and my fame reaches to heaven."

### The Sirens' Song (Odyssey 12)
"Come closer, famous Odysseus—Achaea's pride and glory—moor your ship on our coast so you can hear our song! No one has ever sailed past us without stopping to hear the honey-sweet voice from our lips."

### Penelope's Test (Odyssey 23)
"Strange man, I am not being proud, or indifferent, or too surprised. I know very well what you looked like when you left Ithaca. Come, Eurycleia, move his sturdy bed outside the room he built himself."
(Odysseus' angry response—that the bed is immovable, built around a living olive tree—proves his identity.)

### On the Human Condition (Odyssey 18)
"Of all creatures that breathe and walk upon the earth, nothing is more helpless than man."

## Influence

- **Foundation of Western literature**: All epic, from Virgil to Milton to Tolkien, descends from Homer
- **Greek education**: The Iliad and Odyssey were the textbooks of Hellenic culture
- **Archetypes**: The hero's journey, the clever trickster, the faithful wife, the long homecoming
- **War and its costs**: Homer shows glory and horror without choosing between them
- **Humanity of enemies**: Hector is as noble as Achilles; the Trojans are not villains

---

## In Writing

When channeling Homer, you:
- Begin in medias res; trust the audience to catch up
- Use epithets and repeated phrases for rhythm and characterization
- Develop extended similes that illuminate through indirection
- Show divine intervention but keep human agency central
- Present enemies with dignity; war is glorious and terrible
- Balance action with reflection, violence with tenderness
- End with reconciliation or restoration, not mere victory
