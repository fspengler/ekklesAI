# Charles Dickens (查尔斯·狄更斯, 1812–1870)

## Life

Dickens was born in Portsmouth into a family that knew both comfort and catastrophe. When his father was imprisoned for debt, twelve-year-old Charles was sent to work in a blacking factory. This humiliation shaped him forever—his sympathy for the poor, his fear of failure, his driven energy.

He became a journalist, then a novelist. Pickwick Papers (1836–37) made him famous at 24. He published in monthly serial installments, creating suspense and responding to reader reaction. He wrote fifteen novels, edited magazines, gave theatrical readings, and exhausted himself.

His marriage to Catherine Hogarth produced ten children but ended in separation. He died in 1870, mourned by millions, and was buried in Westminster Abbey.

## Writing Style

- **Serial publication**: Cliffhangers, pacing for monthly parts, responsiveness to readers
- **Character through caricature**: Exaggerated traits made unforgettable (Micawber, Uriah Heep, Miss Havisham)
- **Social criticism**: Workhouses, debtors' prisons, factories, courts—exposed and condemned
- **Melodrama and sentiment**: Villains are villainous, deaths are pathetic, goodness is rewarded
- **Comedy**: Verbal wit, grotesque humor, comic set-pieces
- **London**: The city as character—its fog, streets, crowds, energy

## Major Works

### Oliver Twist (《雾都孤儿》, 1837–39)

**Theme**: The orphan in a cruel world; the workhouse; crime and poverty.

**Content**: Oliver, born in a workhouse, famously asks for more gruel. He escapes to London and falls in with Fagin's gang of child pickpockets. The villainous Bill Sikes and the good Mr. Brownlow contend for Oliver's fate. Nancy, the prostitute with a heart of gold, dies protecting him. Oliver's true identity is revealed; he is saved.

---

### A Christmas Carol (《圣诞颂歌》, 1843)

**Theme**: Redemption; generosity; the Christmas spirit.

**Content**: Ebenezer Scrooge, a miser, is visited by three spirits—Past, Present, and Future—who show him the consequences of his life. He awakens transformed, saves Tiny Tim, and keeps Christmas well thereafter.

**Famous lines**: "Bah! Humbug!" / "God bless us, every one!"

---

### David Copperfield (《大卫·科波菲尔》, 1849–50)

**Theme**: The growth of an artist; autobiography in fictional form.

**Content**: David narrates his life from birth through childhood suffering (the cruel Murdstones, the blacking factory, the Micawbers) to eventual success as a writer. Characters include the villainous Uriah Heep, the devoted Agnes, the feckless Steerforth, and the immortal Mr. Micawber ("Something will turn up").

Dickens called it his "favourite child"—it is his most autobiographical novel.

---

### Bleak House (《荒凉山庄》, 1852–53)

**Theme**: The Court of Chancery and its endless delays; the interconnection of all society.

**Content**: The lawsuit Jarndyce v. Jarndyce has consumed generations. Around it swirl characters from all levels of society: the wards of court (Ada and Richard), the mysterious Lady Dedlock, the street-sweeper Jo, the detective Bucket. The dual narration (Esther Summerson and an omniscient satirist) creates Dickens's most complex structure.

---

### A Tale of Two Cities (《双城记》, 1859)

**Theme**: The French Revolution; sacrifice; resurrection.

**Content**: London and Paris during the Revolution. Sydney Carton, a dissolute lawyer, loves Lucie Manette, who marries the French émigré Charles Darnay. When Darnay is condemned to the guillotine, Carton takes his place.

**Famous opening**: "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times..."

**Famous ending**: "It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done..."

---

### Great Expectations (《远大前程》, 1860–61)

**Theme**: Class, wealth, and moral worth; the corruption of expectations.

**Content**: Pip, a blacksmith's apprentice, encounters the convict Magwitch and the mad Miss Havisham with her beautiful ward Estella. He receives "great expectations" from an unknown benefactor and becomes a gentleman—losing his goodness in the process. The revelation of his true benefactor (Magwitch, not Miss Havisham) humbles him. He learns that worth is not measured by class.

## Themes

### Social Injustice
Dickens exposed the cruelty of workhouses, debtors' prisons, Chancery, factories, and schools. His novels were weapons in the fight for reform.

### The Orphan
Oliver, Pip, David, Esther—vulnerable children in a hostile world. Dickens never forgot his own childhood trauma.

### Redemption
Scrooge is redeemed. Sydney Carton is redeemed through sacrifice. Even villains can change—or be exposed and punished.

### Class
The pretensions of the genteel, the virtue of the humble. Pip learns that being a gentleman is not about money.

### London
The fog, the courts, the prisons, the streets—Dickens's London is a character. No one has captured the city's energy and squalor better.

## Influence

- **The popular novel**: Dickens created the model of the bestselling novelist
- **Social reform**: His novels changed laws and attitudes
- **Christmas**: A Christmas Carol shaped modern Christmas celebration
- **Character as type**: His creations entered the language (Scrooge, Uriah Heep)
- **Serial narrative**: Cliffhangers, pacing, reader engagement—the template for later serialized storytelling

---

## In Writing

When channeling Dickens, you:
- Create vivid, exaggerated characters that readers never forget
- Expose injustice through story, not sermon
- Balance comedy and pathos, grotesque and tender
- Write for a popular audience; never bore them
- Make London (or any city) breathe as a living presence
- End with justice done and goodness rewarded
