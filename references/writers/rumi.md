# Rumi (Jalāl ad-Dīn Muhammad Rūmī, 1207–1273)

## Life

Rumi was born in Balkh (present-day Afghanistan) in 1207. His family fled the Mongol invasions, eventually settling in Konya, in the Seljuk Sultanate of Rum (hence Rumi). His father was a respected theologian and mystic; Rumi inherited his position and became a learned scholar and teacher.

In 1244, his life was transformed by meeting Shams-i-Tabrīzī, a wandering dervish. The encounter ignited Rumi's mystical awakening. The two became inseparable—a scandal to Rumi's students, who eventually drove Shams away (he may have been murdered). Rumi's grief for Shams produced an outpouring of poetry; he signed many poems with Shams's name, as if speaking through his beloved.

After Shams's disappearance, Rumi found other spiritual companions—Salah al-Din Zarkub and Husam al-Din Chalabi—and continued to compose poetry. He founded the Mevlevi Order, known for the whirling dance of the "whirling dervishes."

He died in Konya on December 17, 1273. His shrine remains a site of pilgrimage.

## Writing Style

- **Ghazal**: Short lyric poems of love and mysticism; the signature form
- **Mathnawi couplets**: The long narrative-didactic poem in rhyming couplets
- **Intoxication**: Wine, the tavern, and drunkenness as metaphors for divine ecstasy
- **Paradox**: Dissolution of opposites—pain and joy, absence and presence, self and other
- **Direct address**: Speaking to the Beloved, to the reader, to God, to the self
- **Spontaneity**: Poetry poured out; Rumi dictated while whirling, walking, or in trance
- **Stories within poetry**: Parables, tales, and teaching narratives embedded in verse

## Major Works

### Divan-e Shams-e Tabrizi

**Background**: A vast collection of over 40,000 verses of lyric poetry (ghazals and quatrains), composed after Rumi's transformative encounter with Shams. Many poems are signed with Shams's name.

**Theme**: Divine love; the ecstasy and anguish of the lover seeking the Beloved; the annihilation of the self in union.

**Content**: These are poems of intoxication and longing. The Beloved (Shams, God, the Divine) is addressed, praised, mourned. Wine flows; music plays; the lover burns. Joy and grief intertwine. The self dissolves.

---

### Masnavi-ye Ma'navi (Spiritual Couplets)

**Background**: Rumi's masterwork, composed in his later years at the urging of his disciple Husam al-Din. Over 25,000 couplets in six books. Called "the Quran in Persian" by later Sufis.

**Theme**: Spiritual teaching through story, parable, and commentary; the journey of the soul toward God.

**Content**:

The Masnavi opens with the famous "Song of the Reed" (Ney-nameh): the reed flute laments its separation from the reed bed—a metaphor for the soul's exile from its divine origin.

The six books contain hundreds of stories—some from traditional sources, some invented—interrupted by commentary, digressions, and spiritual explication. Stories include:
- The king and the handmaiden
- The elephant in the dark room
- Moses and the shepherd
- The man who knocked on his friend's door
- The parrot and the merchant

Each story unfolds meanings at multiple levels.

---

### Fihi Ma Fihi (Discourses)

**Background**: Prose records of Rumi's talks and teachings, collected by his disciples.

**Theme**: Spiritual guidance; explanations of Sufi concepts; responses to questions.

**Content**: More accessible than the poetry; direct teaching on love, prayer, the ego, and the path.

---

### Majalis-e Sab'a (Seven Sessions)

Seven sermons delivered by Rumi, combining Quranic interpretation with mystical teaching.

## Famous Poems and Passages

### The Song of the Reed (Opening of the Masnavi)
"Listen to the reed, how it tells its story,
Complaining of separation:
'Ever since they cut me from the reed bed,
My cry has made men and women weep.
I want a breast torn by separation
That I may speak of the pain of longing.
Everyone who is far from their source
Yearns for the time when they were one with it.'"

### The Guest House
"This being human is a guest house.
Every morning a new arrival.
A joy, a depression, a meanness,
some momentary awareness comes
as an unexpected visitor.
Welcome and entertain them all!
Even if they're a crowd of sorrows,
who violently sweep your house
empty of its furniture,
still, treat each guest honorably.
He may be clearing you out
for some new delight."

### On Love
"If you find me not within you,
you will never find me.
For I have been with you
from the beginning of me."

### The Wound
"Don't turn away. Keep your gaze on the bandaged place.
That's where the light enters you."

### On Union
"Lovers don't finally meet somewhere.
They're in each other all along."

### On Silence
"Silence is the language of God;
all else is poor translation."

### On the Self
"You are not a drop in the ocean.
You are the entire ocean in a drop."

### On Seeking
"What you seek is seeking you."

### On Transformation
"Be melting snow.
Wash yourself of yourself."

### On Death
"When I die, don't say he died.
Say he was dead, and came to life,
and was taken by the Beloved."

### On the Intellect
"Sell your cleverness and buy bewilderment.
Cleverness is mere opinion; bewilderment is intuition."

### Knock on the Door
"I said, 'Who's there?'
The Beloved said, 'It is I.'
I said, 'Go away.'
Years later, another knock.
'Who's there?'
'It is you.'
The door opened."

### The Field
"Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing,
there is a field. I'll meet you there.
When the soul lies down in that grass,
the world is too full to talk about.
Ideas, language, even the phrase 'each other'
doesn't make any sense."

### Dance
"Dance, when you're broken open.
Dance, if you've torn the bandage off.
Dance in the middle of the fighting.
Dance in your blood.
Dance when you're perfectly free."

### Love's Journey
"Love is the bridge between you and everything."

### On the Heart
"Your heart knows the way. Run in that direction."

## Themes

### Divine Love (Ishq)
Rumi's central theme. Love is not merely emotion but cosmic force—the energy that moves the universe. The lover seeks annihilation in the Beloved.

### Separation and Longing
The soul is separated from its source; all longing is ultimately for return to God. The reed's lament is the soul's lament.

### Intoxication and Ecstasy
Wine, the tavern, drunkenness—metaphors for the ecstasy of divine encounter. Reason is left at the door.

### The Annihilation of Self (Fana)
The ego must die for the soul to live. The drop dissolves into the ocean. "Die before you die."

### Unity of Being
All apparent multiplicity is one reality. The Beloved is not other than the lover. Separation is illusion.

### The Path (Tariqa)
Sufi discipline: prayer, fasting, meditation, whirling, and above all, love as the method of transformation.

## Influence

- **Best-selling poet in America**: In translation, Rumi became a phenomenon—beloved for his universal themes of love and longing
- **Sufi tradition**: The Mevlevi Order (whirling dervishes) continues his practice
- **Cross-cultural appeal**: Rumi is read by Muslims, Christians, secularists, New Age seekers
- **Modern poetry**: His directness, his metaphors, his emotional intensity influence contemporary poets
- **Translation debates**: Much popular Rumi in English is heavily adapted; scholarly translations differ

## In Writing

When channeling Rumi, you:
- Speak of love as the supreme reality
- Use paradox to dissolve ordinary thinking
- Address the Beloved directly—intimate, urgent, longing
- Employ intoxication metaphors: wine, tavern, drunkenness
- Tell stories that open into meaning at multiple levels
- Dissolve the boundary between self and other
- Welcome pain as the opening for light
- Let the poem move through you; do not compose—receive
- Remember: the apparent separation from God is the wound; love is the healing
