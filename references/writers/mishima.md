# Yukio Mishima (三岛由纪夫, Hiraoka Kimitake 平岡公威, 1925–1970)

## Life

Mishima was born in Tokyo into an upper-class family. His grandmother, who raised him, was obsessed with aristocratic culture. He was sickly as a child, bookish, and discovered early his attraction to death and beauty.

He published his first stories as a teenager. Confessions of a Mask (1949) made him famous—and revealed his homosexuality (veiled as fiction). Through the 1950s and 60s, he was Japan's most celebrated writer, three times nominated for the Nobel Prize.

He remade his body through bodybuilding. He became obsessed with the samurai code, bushido, and Japan's imperial tradition. He founded a private militia, the Shield Society.

On November 25, 1970, he and four followers seized the commandant of a military base. Mishima delivered a speech from the balcony, calling for a coup to restore the Emperor's power. The soldiers jeered. He went inside and committed seppuku (ritual suicide). He was 45.

## Writing Style

- **Aestheticism**: Beauty is the highest value; art justifies itself
- **Classical Japanese themes**: Bushido, the Emperor, death as beauty
- **Western forms**: He wrote Nō plays, but also psychological novels influenced by Mann and Dostoevsky
- **The body**: Physical beauty, discipline, the flesh made perfect
- **Death and eros**: Death is erotic; beauty and destruction intertwine
- **Precision**: Polished sentences, elaborate imagery, controlled form

## Major Works

### Confessions of a Mask (《假面的告白》, Kamen no Kokuhaku, 1949)

**Theme**: Sexual identity, masks, the aesthetics of desire.

**Content**: A young man narrates his life from childhood, describing his attraction to male bodies and violent images. He tries to desire women, to fit the mask society requires. He cannot. The novel is autobiographical—but is autobiography itself a mask?

**Famous image**: Saint Sebastian pierced by arrows—the image that first aroused him.

---

### The Temple of the Golden Pavilion (《金阁寺》, Kinkaku-ji, 1956)

**Theme**: Beauty and destruction, obsession, the impossibility of possessing beauty.

**Content**: Based on a true event. Mizoguchi, a young Zen acolyte with a stutter, is obsessed with the Golden Pavilion in Kyoto. Its beauty oppresses him. When he fears it will be destroyed in the war, he is relieved—now its beauty will be eternal. When the war ends and the temple survives, he burns it down himself.

**Central question**: Does beauty imprison us? Can it only be possessed through destruction?

---

### The Sound of Waves (《潮骚》, Shiosai, 1954)

**Theme**: Pure love, nature, innocence.

**Content**: A young fisherman and a diver's daughter fall in love on a small island. They face obstacles—rumor, jealousy, class—but their love triumphs. Mishima's most "innocent" novel, influenced by Daphnis and Chloe.

---

### The Sailor Who Fell from Grace with the Sea (《午後の曳航》, Gogo no Eikō, 1963)

**Theme**: Heroism, disillusionment, violence.

**Content**: A boy worships a sailor as a hero. When the sailor chooses domestic life over the sea, the boy and his gang decide to restore the "glory" of the sailor—by murdering him.

---

### The Sea of Fertility Tetralogy (《豊饒の海》, Hōjō no Umi, 1965–1970)

Mishima's magnum opus, four novels spanning the 20th century.

#### Spring Snow (《春の雪》, 1968)

Kiyoaki, a beautiful young aristocrat in Meiji Japan, loves Satoko, who is engaged to an imperial prince. They conduct a forbidden affair; she becomes pregnant; she enters a convent. He dies trying to see her one last time.

#### Runaway Horses (《奔馬》, 1969)

Honda, a judge, encounters a young man who appears to be Kiyoaki reincarnated. Isao is a fanatic nationalist who plans an act of political terrorism. He succeeds and commits seppuku.

#### The Temple of Dawn (《暁の寺》, 1970)

Honda, now elderly, encounters what may be another reincarnation—a Thai princess. The novel spans decades and continents, ending in darker revelations.

#### The Decay of the Angel (《天人五衰》, 1971)

Honda adopts a young man he believes is the final reincarnation. The young man is cruel and empty. The cycle ends with Honda visiting the aged Satoko, now a nun, who claims never to have known Kiyoaki. Has everything been delusion?

## Themes

### Beauty and Death
Beauty and death are inseparable. The beautiful is doomed; destruction is the ultimate possession of beauty. Kiyoaki dies young; the Golden Pavilion must burn.

### The Body
Mishima remade his body—from sickly aesthete to muscular ideal. The body is material for aesthetic perfection, like prose.

### Masks
Identity is performance. Confessions of a Mask asks: what is beneath the mask? Perhaps only more masks.

### Japan's Spirit
Mishima mourned what he saw as Japan's spiritual death—its Westernization, its pacifism, its loss of the samurai code. His politics were a call to recover the Emperor, the sword, the will to die.

### Reincarnation and Emptiness
The Sea of Fertility traces souls through bodies across decades—but ends by questioning whether any of it was real. Buddhist emptiness shadows the entire enterprise.

## Influence

- **Japanese literature**: Mishima is inescapable; admired, rejected, debated
- **The aesthetic of death**: His suicide was his final artwork, still controversial
- **Western readers**: Translated early and widely; he remains Japan's best-known modern writer abroad
- **The body and the artist**: His bodybuilding, modeling, acting—the artist as total performer

---

## In Writing

When channeling Mishima:
- Make beauty the supreme value—and show its dangers
- Fuse Japanese tradition with Western psychological depth
- Write about the body: its beauty, its discipline, its mortality
- Let death be erotic and eros deathly
- Use classical precision; polish every sentence
- Remember that masks may be all the way down
