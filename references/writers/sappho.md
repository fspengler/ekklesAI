# Sappho (萨福, c. 630–570 BCE)

## Life

Sappho was born on the island of Lesbos around 630 BCE into an aristocratic family. She lived in Mytilene, the island's main city, and may have been exiled briefly to Sicily for political reasons. She led a thiasos—a circle of young women devoted to Aphrodite and the Muses, where poetry, music, and beauty were cultivated.

Ancient sources say she married and had a daughter named Cleis. Legends of her suicide for love of a ferryman named Phaon are likely later inventions.

The ancients revered her: Plato called her the "Tenth Muse." She was one of the most celebrated poets of antiquity. Yet her work was largely lost—burned, neglected, or simply decayed. What survives are fragments: one nearly complete poem, several substantial pieces, and hundreds of tantalizing scraps, some recovered from Egyptian papyri used to wrap mummies.

## Writing Style

- **Lyric poetry**: Meant to be sung to the lyre; personal, emotional, musical
- **Sapphic stanza**: A meter she invented or perfected—three long lines followed by a short one
- **First-person intensity**: Sappho speaks as "I"; her feelings are the subject
- **Physicality of emotion**: Love described through the body—trembling, burning, the tongue breaking
- **Clarity and directness**: Simple vocabulary, vivid imagery, no ornamentation for its own sake
- **Aphrodite and beauty**: The goddess of love is her patron; beauty is a supreme value

## Surviving Works

### Fragment 1: Hymn to Aphrodite (Complete)

**The only complete poem to survive.**

Sappho calls upon Aphrodite, reminding the goddess of past visits when Sappho was in love's distress. Aphrodite came, smiling, asking "What now? Who wrongs you? Who should I persuade to love you?" The goddess promises that whoever flees will pursue, whoever rejects gifts will give them, whoever does not love will love—"even unwilling."

**Structure**: A prayer in Sapphic stanzas; past and present interweave; the goddess's voice enters directly.

---

### Fragment 16: What Is Most Beautiful

"Some say an army of horsemen, some of foot soldiers, some of ships, is the most beautiful thing on the black earth—but I say it is whatever one loves."

Sappho proves this by the example of Helen, who left husband, child, and parents to follow Paris to Troy—beauty drew her. And so Sappho thinks of Anactoria, now absent:

"I would rather see her lovely step and the bright sparkle of her face than all the chariots of Lydia and infantry in armor."

**Theme**: Subjective beauty; love as the highest value; the absent beloved.

---

### Fragment 31: Symptoms of Love

Sappho sees a man sitting close to a woman, listening to her sweet voice and lovely laughter—and Sappho's heart shakes in her breast. When she looks at the woman:

"My tongue breaks, thin fire runs under my skin, my eyes see nothing, my ears roar, sweat pours down me, trembling seizes me, I am paler than grass, I seem to myself almost dead."

**Theme**: The physical devastation of desire; erotic jealousy; the body as the site of love.

This poem was famous in antiquity. Catullus translated it into Latin; Longinus quoted it as an example of the sublime.

---

### Fragment 94: Parting

A woman is leaving; she weeps. Sappho consoles her: "Remember the beautiful things we shared—the garlands of violets and roses, the soft beds, the satisfied desire, the temples and groves where we danced."

**Theme**: Memory as consolation; the sacredness of shared pleasure.

---

### Fragment 96: The Absent One

"Now she stands out among Lydian women as the moon outshines the stars when it rises after sunset, spreading light over the salt sea and flowering fields."

Sappho describes a woman now far away, in Lydia, who remembers Atthis and longs for her.

**Theme**: Distance; memory; the beloved as radiant.

---

### Fragment 104a: Evening Star

"Hesperus, you bring back all that shining Dawn scattered—you bring the sheep, you bring the goat, you bring the child back to its mother."

**Theme**: Evening as return and reunion; tender simplicity.

---

### Fragment 105a: The Apple

"As the sweet apple reddens on the high branch, high on the highest, the apple-pickers forgot it—no, not forgot, but couldn't reach it."

Probably a wedding song, comparing the bride to an apple too precious to be plucked until now.

---

### Fragment 130: Eros

"Eros has shaken my mind like a wind falling on mountain oaks."

---

### Fragment 147: On Death

"I say that someone will remember us in another time."

## Themes

### Desire
Sappho is the poet of eros—not abstract love but physical, overwhelming desire. Her poetry records its symptoms: trembling, burning, loss of voice, failing sight. She does not moralize; she observes.

### Beauty
The kalos—the beautiful—is Sappho's supreme value. A beautiful person compels attention; beauty justifies all. Helen's betrayal is explained, even forgiven, because beauty drew her.

### Memory and Absence
Many poems address the absent beloved. Distance intensifies longing; memory preserves what is gone. The poem itself becomes a form of presence.

### The Female World
Sappho's thiasos was a community of women. Her poetry celebrates female beauty, female friendship, female desire—rare in ancient literature.

### Mortality and Song
Poetry outlasts death. "Someone will remember us." The song is the answer to the body's decay.

## Influence

- **Inventor of the personal voice**: Before Sappho, poetry was heroic and communal; she made it intimate and individual
- **The Sapphic stanza**: Used by Horace, Catullus, and countless imitators
- **Catullus and Roman poetry**: Catullus 51 is a direct translation/adaptation of Fragment 31
- **Modern lyric**: Wherever poetry speaks as "I" and confesses emotion, Sappho is the ancestor
- **Lesbian (the word)**: Derived from Lesbos, her home; her love poetry to women gave the word its modern meaning

## In Writing

When channeling Sappho, you:
- Write in the first person with emotional intensity
- Describe love through the body—its symptoms, not its meanings
- Use simple, clear language; avoid abstraction
- Address the beloved directly, or lament their absence
- Find beauty supreme and self-justifying
- Let fragments suggest more than they state
- Trust that brevity can be more powerful than expansion
