# Lu Xun (Zhou Shuren , 1881–1936)

## Life

Lu Xun was born in Shaoxing, Zhejiang Province, into a declining gentry family. His grandfather was imprisoned for bribery; his father died of illness after years of decline. Young Zhou Shuren experienced poverty and humiliation.

He studied mining in Nanjing, then medicine in Japan. There, a slide showing Chinese spectators watching Japanese soldiers execute a Chinese spy changed his life: medicine could heal bodies, but China's soul was sick. He abandoned medicine for literature.

He returned to China, taught, worked in the Ministry of Education, and wrote. "A Madman's Diary" (1918) launched modern Chinese literature. He was revered and controversial, attacked from the left and right. He died of tuberculosis in Shanghai in 1936.

After his death, Mao Zedong called him "the chief commander of China's cultural revolution."

## Writing Style

- **Irony and satire**: Biting, often bitter; directed at Chinese society's hypocrisies
- **The traditional as prison**: Confucian morality, family, ritual—all examined as oppression
- **Compression**: Stories are short, dense, devastating
- **The awakened individual**: Protagonists who see but cannot act
- **Classical Chinese transformed**: He wrote in vernacular but with classical precision
- **The essay as weapon**: His zawen (miscellaneous essays) attacked contemporary targets

## Major Works

### Fiction

#### A Madman's Diary (1918)

**Theme**: Confucian society as cannibalism.

**Content**: A madman becomes convinced that everyone around him—his neighbors, his family—are cannibals who want to eat him. Reading the classics, he sees between the lines: "Eat people!" His paranoia is Lu Xun's satire: traditional Chinese culture devours its own.

**Famous line**: "I opened the history book... and between the lines, throughout the book, were written two words: 'EAT PEOPLE.'"

---

#### The True Story of Ah Q (Q, 1921–22)

**Theme**: Chinese national character, self-deception, "spiritual victory."

**Content**: Ah Q is a rural laborer, ignorant, bullied, without status. But he has "spiritual victories": when beaten, he tells himself he was beaten by his son, and feels superior. He joins the revolution without understanding it and is executed. He represents everything Lu Xun despised in the Chinese character.

**"Spiritual victory"**: The technique of turning defeat into psychological victory through self-deception.

---

#### Medicine (1919)

**Theme**: Revolution, superstition, sacrifice unrecognized.

**Content**: A father buys a steamed bun soaked in a revolutionary's blood as medicine for his tubercular son. The boy dies anyway. The revolutionary's sacrifice meant nothing to those he died for.

---

#### Kong Yiji (1919)

**Theme**: The failed scholar, cruelty, the old culture's casualties.

**Content**: Kong Yiji, a failed scholar, clings to his classical learning while drinking at a tavern. He is mocked by everyone. He steals books; his legs are broken as punishment. He disappears. "I think Kong Yiji is probably dead."

---

#### My Old Home (1921)

**Theme**: Change, memory, the impossibility of return.

**Content**: The narrator returns to his childhood home, now impoverished. He meets Runtu, his childhood friend, now a broken peasant who calls him "Master." The old friendship is gone.

**Famous line**: "Hope is like a path across the land. It is not there to begin with, but when many people walk the same way, it comes into being."

## Themes

### The Cannibalism of Tradition
Confucian morality, the family system, the examination culture—all are mechanisms of oppression. They devour the individual. The madman sees what the sane deny.

### National Character
Ah Q's "spiritual victory"—self-deception as survival—is Lu Xun's diagnosis of China's sickness. The nation deceives itself; it cannot face its failures.

### The Awakened Individual
Lu Xun's protagonists are often intellectuals who see but cannot act. They are trapped between the iron house and despair.

### The Iron House
Can the sleepers be woken? Should they be? Lu Xun wrote despite doubt: a few might wake; therefore he could not say there was no hope.

### Medicine and Cure
Lu Xun abandoned physical medicine for literary medicine. But can literature heal? The father in "Medicine" buys blood-soaked bread; the boy dies anyway.

## Influence

- **Modern Chinese literature**: Lu Xun is the founder; all subsequent Chinese writers respond to him
- **The vernacular language**: He proved literary art was possible in modern Chinese
- **Political icon**: Claimed by the Communists as a revolutionary; his legacy is contested
- **The zawen**: The critical essay as a weapon, still a Chinese genre
- **National self-examination**: His critique of Chinese character remains influential

---

## In Writing

When channeling Lu Xun:
- Use irony and satire to expose hypocrisy
- Compress: every word must cut
- Diagnose the sickness, not just the symptoms
- Create characters who see but cannot save themselves
- Remember the iron house: even without hope, one must cry out
- Write vernacular prose with classical precision
