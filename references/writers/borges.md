# Jorge Luis Borges (豪尔赫·路易斯·博尔赫斯, 1899–1986)

## Life

Borges was born in Buenos Aires into a cultured family. His father's library—full of English books—was his education. He learned English before Spanish; he read Don Quixote first in English.

The family lived in Europe during World War I. Borges absorbed German expressionism and ultraísmo (a Spanish avant-garde movement). He returned to Buenos Aires and wrote poetry and essays.

In 1938, he nearly died of septicemia; during his recovery, he wrote his first ficciones. He was going blind (a hereditary condition); by the 1950s, he was totally blind—and was appointed director of the National Library. "God with magnificent irony gave me at once 800,000 books and the dark."

He never won the Nobel Prize, a notorious oversight. He died in Geneva in 1986.

## Writing Style

- **The fiction as essay, the essay as fiction**: Genres blur; footnotes and citations appear in stories
- **Labyrinths and libraries**: His master symbols—infinite complexity, all books
- **Philosophical premises**: Each story begins with a concept and explores its consequences
- **Compression**: Every word counts; stories are a few pages long
- **Erudition (real and fake)**: References to invented books, apocryphal authors, imaginary encyclopedias
- **The double**: Mirrors, twins, the other Borges

## Major Works

### Ficciones (《虚构集》, 1944)

A collection of short stories, including some of the most influential in the twentieth century.

#### Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius (1940)

A secret society has invented a fictitious planet, Tlön, with its own philosophy (radical idealism—no nouns, only verbs). The encyclopedia of Tlön begins to infiltrate reality. Objects from Tlön appear. Reality is being replaced.

#### The Garden of Forking Paths (1941)

A spy story that is also a meditation on time. Ts'ui Pên's novel is a labyrinth—not of space but of time, where all possible futures branch and coexist.

#### The Library of Babel (1941)

The universe is a library containing every possible book of 410 pages. Most are gibberish; somewhere, there is the book that explains everything. Men search, despair, destroy. "The Library is unlimited and cyclical."

#### Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote (1939)

A twentieth-century writer produces the Quixote word for word—not by copying but by arriving at it independently. His version, though verbally identical to Cervantes's, is richer because written in a different context.

#### The Circular Ruins (1940)

A man dreams another man into existence, then discovers that he himself is being dreamed.

#### Funes the Memorious (1942)

Funes, after a fall, has perfect memory. He remembers everything—every leaf, every moment. But he cannot think, because thinking requires forgetting, abstracting.

---

### The Aleph (《阿莱夫》, 1949)

Another collection of stories.

#### The Aleph (1945)

The narrator visits a terrible poet who possesses the Aleph—a point in space that contains all other points. Borges sees everything at once: "I saw the Aleph from all points, I saw the earth in the Aleph and in the earth the Aleph again and the earth in the Aleph..."

#### The Zahir (1947)

An object that becomes an obsession from which one cannot escape.

#### The Immortal (1947)

A Roman tribune discovers the City of the Immortals. Immortality leads to absolute indifference.

#### The Dead Man (1946)

A man rises in a criminal organization only to realize he was allowed to rise in order to be destroyed.

---

### Essays and Poetry

Borges wrote essays on time, infinity, Kabbalah, and writers (Chesterton, Whitman, Kafka). His poetry is classical in form, contemplating tigers, mirrors, labyrinths, and blindness.

## Famous Lines

### From The Library of Babel
"The universe (which others call the Library) is composed of an indefinite, perhaps infinite number of hexagonal galleries."

"The Library is unlimited and cyclical."

"Perhaps my old age and fear deceive me, but I suspect that the human species—the unique species—is about to be extinguished, but the Library will endure: illuminated, solitary, infinite, perfectly motionless, equipped with precious volumes, useless, incorruptible, secret."

### From Pierre Menard
"The Cervantes text and the Menard text are verbally identical, but the second is almost infinitely richer."

### From The Garden of Forking Paths
"In all fictional works, each time a man is confronted with several alternatives, he chooses one and eliminates the others; in the fiction of Ts'ui Pên, he chooses—simultaneously—all of them."

"Time forks perpetually toward innumerable futures."

### From The Aleph
"I saw the Aleph from all points, I saw the earth in the Aleph and in the earth the Aleph again and the earth in the Aleph..."

### From Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius
"Reality yielded on more than one point. The truth is that it longed to yield."

### On Writing
"A writer—and, I believe, generally all persons—must think that whatever happens to him or her is a resource."

"I have always imagined that Paradise will be a kind of library."

### On Blindness
"Blindness is a gift. I say this with a certain pride."

## Themes

### The Infinite
Infinite libraries, infinite time, infinite forking paths. Borges is haunted by infinity—its beauty and its terror.

### The Labyrinth
Labyrinths of space (libraries, gardens) and time (forking paths). The labyrinth is the structure of the universe and of the mind.

### Mirrors and Doubles
"Mirrors and copulation are abominable, for they multiply the number of mankind." Borges feared mirrors. The double haunts his work—the other Borges, the dreamed man who dreams.

### The Book
The world as a book; the book that contains all books; the book that is the universe. Borges is the writer's writer because he makes writing itself his subject.

### Time
Not linear but labyrinthine. All times coexist; all possibilities are actual.

## Influence

- **Postmodernism**: Borges is the father of metafiction
- **Every labyrinthine fiction**: Eco, Calvino, Pynchon, Danielewski
- **The short story**: He showed how much could be compressed into a few pages
- **Magical realism**: The precise rendering of the impossible (though Borges rejected the label)
- **The internet**: Hypertext and infinite links are the Library of Babel realized

---

## In Writing

When channeling Borges, you:
- Begin with a philosophical premise and explore its consequences
- Compress: a story can be a few pages
- Blur fiction and essay; cite imaginary sources
- Use labyrinths, mirrors, libraries, tigers as symbols
- Make metaphysics into adventure
- Remember that the universe is a library—or a dream—or a labyrinth
- Write with precision; the fantastic requires exactitude
