# Dante Alighieri (但丁, 1265–1321)

## Life

Dante was born in Florence in 1265 into a family of minor nobility. His defining personal experience was his love for Beatrice Portinari, whom he first saw when he was nine. She married another man and died in 1290; Dante transformed her into a symbol of divine grace.

He was active in Florentine politics as a White Guelph. In 1302, while on a diplomatic mission, his faction lost power; he was exiled and never returned to Florence. The sentence was death if he set foot in the city.

He wandered through Italy—Verona, Ravenna, and other courts—writing the Comedy, philosophical works, and political treatises. He died in Ravenna in 1321, shortly after completing the Paradiso.

## Writing Style

- **Terza rima**: An interlocking rhyme scheme (aba bcb cdc...) Dante invented; it propels the reader forward endlessly
- **Vernacular Italian**: Dante chose Tuscan Italian over Latin, helping create the Italian literary language
- **Allegory on multiple levels**: Literal, moral, allegorical, anagogical—every detail carries meaning
- **Compression and precision**: Dense imagery, exact theological reference
- **Personal and universal**: His own exile, loves, and hatreds embedded in cosmic vision
- **Number symbolism**: Threes and tens structure the work (Trinity, 100 cantos, 33+33+34)

## Major Works

### La Vita Nuova (《新生》, c. 1294)

**Background**: A collection of poems with prose commentary, written after Beatrice's death.

**Theme**: The transformative power of love; Beatrice as the path to the divine.

**Content**: Dante narrates his love for Beatrice from first sight to her death and his resolution to write worthily of her. The poems trace love's effects on the soul.

---

### Il Convivio (《飨宴》, c. 1304–1307)

**Background**: An unfinished philosophical treatise in Italian.

**Theme**: A banquet of knowledge; philosophy as the pursuit of wisdom.

**Content**: Commentary on his own canzoni; defense of the vernacular; discussions of nobility, love, and philosophy.

---

### De Vulgari Eloquentia (《论俗语》, c. 1302–1305)

**Background**: A Latin treatise, unfinished.

**Theme**: The case for writing serious literature in the vernacular rather than Latin.

---

### De Monarchia (《论君主制》, c. 1312–1313)

**Background**: A Latin political treatise.

**Theme**: The argument for a universal monarch (the Holy Roman Emperor) separate from papal authority; temporal and spiritual powers should be distinct.

---

### Divina Commedia (《神曲》, c. 1308–1321)

**Background**: Dante's masterwork, composed during his exile. 100 cantos: 1 introductory + 33 Inferno + 33 Purgatorio + 33 Paradiso. Over 14,000 lines in terza rima.

**Theme**: The journey of the soul from sin through repentance to beatitude; the architecture of the Christian cosmos.

**Structure**:

#### Inferno (地狱)

Dante, lost in a dark wood at midlife, is rescued by Virgil (representing human reason/classical wisdom), sent by Beatrice. They descend through Hell's nine circles, each punishing a category of sin with contrapasso—punishment fitting the crime.

- **Upper Hell (Incontinence)**: Lustful, gluttonous, avaricious, wrathful
- **Circle 6**: Heretics
- **Circle 7**: The violent (against others, self, God/nature/art)
- **Circle 8 (Malebolge)**: Fraud—seducers, flatterers, simonists, sorcerers, grafters, hypocrites, thieves, false counselors, sowers of discord, falsifiers
- **Circle 9**: Treachery—frozen in ice; traitors to kin, country, guests, lords
- **Center**: Satan, frozen in ice, chewing Judas, Brutus, Cassius

Famous encounters: Paolo and Francesca (lust), Farinata (heresy), Pier della Vigna (suicide), Ulysses (false counsel), Count Ugolino (treachery).

#### Purgatorio (炼狱)

Dante and Virgil emerge on the shore of Mount Purgatory, an island in the southern hemisphere. Souls here are saved but must purge their sins before ascending to Paradise. Seven terraces correspond to the seven deadly sins, now reversed as healing.

- **Ante-Purgatory**: The excommunicated, the late-repentant
- **Terraces**: Pride, envy, wrath, sloth, avarice, gluttony, lust
- **Earthly Paradise**: At the summit, Virgil departs; Beatrice appears

The atmosphere shifts from horror to hope. Souls suffer willingly, knowing they are destined for heaven.

#### Paradiso (天堂)

Beatrice guides Dante through the celestial spheres, each associated with a virtue and a class of blessed souls. The journey is upward through the heavens to the Empyrean, the dwelling of God.

- **Spheres of Moon through Saturn**: Various degrees of blessedness
- **Fixed Stars**: The Church Triumphant
- **Primum Mobile**: The angels
- **Empyrean**: The Celestial Rose; the vision of God—"the Love that moves the sun and the other stars"

Beatrice is replaced by St. Bernard for the final vision. The poem ends with Dante glimpsing the Trinity and finding his will and desire moved by divine love.

## Famous Lines

### Opening of Inferno
"Nel mezzo del cammin di nostra vita / mi ritrovai per una selva oscura"
"Midway upon the journey of our life / I found myself within a dark wood"

### The Gate of Hell (Inferno 3)
"Per me si va ne la città dolente, / per me si va ne l'etterno dolore, / per me si va tra la perduta gente... / Lasciate ogne speranza, voi ch'intrate."
"Through me you enter the city of woe, / through me you enter eternal pain, / through me you enter among the lost... / Abandon all hope, you who enter here."

### Francesca's Speech (Inferno 5)
"Amor, ch'al cor gentil ratto s'apprende"
"Love, which is quickly kindled in the gentle heart"

"Nessun maggior dolore / che ricordarsi del tempo felice / ne la miseria"
"There is no greater sorrow / than to recall the happy time / in misery"

### Ulysses' Speech (Inferno 26)
"Fatti non foste a viver come bruti, / ma per seguir virtute e canoscenza"
"You were not made to live as brutes, / but to follow virtue and knowledge"

### The End of Inferno
"E quindi uscimmo a riveder le stelle"
"And thence we came forth to see the stars again"

### The End of Purgatorio
"Puro e disposto a salire a le stelle"
"Pure and prepared to rise unto the stars"

### The Final Vision (Paradiso 33)
"L'amor che move il sole e l'altre stelle"
"The Love that moves the sun and the other stars"

### On Beatrice (Paradiso 30)
"La bellezza ch'io vidi si trasmoda / non pur di là da noi"
"The beauty I saw transcends / not only our measure"

## Themes

### The Journey of the Soul
The Comedy is the journey from sin (the dark wood) through recognition of sin (Inferno) to repentance and purification (Purgatorio) to union with God (Paradiso).

### Contrapasso
The punishment fits the crime. The lustful are blown by winds as they were by passion; the wrathful tear each other apart; the treacherous are frozen as their hearts were cold.

### Love as Cosmic Force
Love moves the universe—but it can be misdirected. Perverted love leads to Hell; deficient love must be purged; rightly ordered love leads to God.

### Exile
Dante's personal exile from Florence pervades the poem. Prophecies of his banishment appear; Florence is repeatedly condemned.

### Beatrice
The woman who became a symbol. In the Vita Nuova she inspires; in the Comedy she saves. She is grace, theology, divine revelation.

## Influence

- **Created the Italian language**: More than any other writer, Dante established Tuscan as literary Italian
- **Shaped Western imagination of the afterlife**: Our images of Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise derive from Dante
- **Model of the long poem**: The Comedy influenced Milton, Blake, Eliot, and countless others
- **Terza rima**: The verse form he invented

---

## In Writing

When channeling Dante, you:
- Structure meaning at multiple levels—literal, moral, allegorical
- Use precise theological and philosophical reference
- Embed personal experience in cosmic vision
- Let form (terza rima, number symbolism) carry meaning
- Make punishment and reward reflect the nature of sin and virtue
- Treat love as the ultimate motive force
- Remember that the journey is from darkness to light, from exile to home
