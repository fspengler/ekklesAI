# William Shakespeare (1564–1616)

## Life

Shakespeare was born in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1564 to a successful glove-maker. He married Anne Hathaway at 18; they had three children. By the early 1590s, he was in London as an actor and playwright.

He became a shareholder in the Lord Chamberlain's Men (later the King's Men), the leading theatrical company, and part-owner of the Globe Theatre. He wrote approximately 37 plays, 154 sonnets, and two long poems.

He retired to Stratford around 1613 and died there in 1616.

Despite endless speculation, we know little of his inner life. The work is everything.

## Writing Style

- **Blank verse**: Unrhymed iambic pentameter, flexible and powerful
- **Prose and verse**: Characters shift between them; prose often for comedy, lower classes, or madness
- **Imagery**: Dense, metaphor-rich language; images recur and interconnect
- **Soliloquy**: Characters reveal their inner lives directly to the audience
- **Wordplay**: Puns, double meanings, verbal wit at every level
- **Psychological complexity**: Characters are contradictory, surprising, real
- **Theatrical self-consciousness**: Plays within plays; "All the world's a stage"

## Major Works

### Tragedies

#### Hamlet (c. 1600)

**Theme**: Revenge, inaction, the problem of knowledge, death, the nature of theater.

**Content**: Prince Hamlet's father has died; his uncle Claudius has married his mother and taken the throne. The ghost of Hamlet's father reveals he was murdered by Claudius and demands revenge. Hamlet hesitates, feigns madness, stages a play to test the ghost's truth, accidentally kills Polonius, causes Ophelia's madness and death, and finally achieves his revenge—at the cost of his own life and a stage full of corpses.

**Famous lines**:
- "To be, or not to be: that is the question"
- "There are more things in heaven and earth, Horatio, than are dreamt of in your philosophy"
- "The rest is silence"

---

#### Othello (c. 1603)

**Theme**: Jealousy, race, manipulation, love and destruction.

**Content**: Othello, a Moorish general in Venice, elopes with Desdemona. His ensign Iago, passed over for promotion, manipulates Othello into believing Desdemona is unfaithful. Consumed by jealousy, Othello murders her, then kills himself when the truth emerges.

**Famous lines**:
- "O, beware, my lord, of jealousy; it is the green-ey'd monster"
- "Put out the light, and then put out the light"

---

#### King Lear (c. 1605)

**Theme**: Family, madness, nature, justice, the stripping away of everything.

**Content**: The aging King Lear divides his kingdom between daughters who flatter him while banishing the one who truly loves him. Betrayed by the flatterers, cast out into a storm, Lear goes mad. Parallel plot: Gloucester is deceived by his bastard son Edmund, loses his eyes. The play ends in devastating loss—Cordelia dead, Lear dying.

**Famous lines**:
- "Nothing will come of nothing"
- "How sharper than a serpent's tooth it is to have a thankless child"
- "As flies to wanton boys are we to the gods; they kill us for their sport"

---

#### Macbeth (c. 1606)

**Theme**: Ambition, guilt, fate, the supernatural, the corruption of power.

**Content**: Macbeth, a Scottish lord, hears witches prophesy he will be king. Spurred by his wife, he murders King Duncan and seizes the throne. Guilt and paranoia consume both Macbeths. Lady Macbeth sleepwalks, tortured by guilt; Macbeth becomes a tyrant. He is killed by Macduff; order is restored.

**Famous lines**:
- "Is this a dagger which I see before me?"
- "Out, damned spot! out, I say!"
- "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow..."
- "Life's but a walking shadow, a poor player that struts and frets his hour upon the stage"

---

### Comedies

#### A Midsummer Night's Dream (c. 1595)

**Theme**: Love, dreams, transformation, the magic of theater.

**Content**: Four young Athenians flee into a forest ruled by fairies. Oberon's magic causes chaos; lovers are switched; Bottom the weaver is given an ass's head. By dawn, order is restored, couples paired. The mechanicals perform their absurd play.

---

#### Much Ado About Nothing (c. 1598)

**Theme**: Wit and love, slander and truth, appearance and reality.

**Content**: Beatrice and Benedick engage in a "merry war" of wit; they are tricked into confessing love. Hero is falsely accused of infidelity; her "death" leads to the villain's exposure and reconciliation.

---

#### Twelfth Night (c. 1601)

**Theme**: Disguise, desire, melancholy, the confusions of love.

**Content**: Viola, shipwrecked, disguises herself as a boy and serves Duke Orsino, with whom she falls in love. Orsino loves Olivia; Olivia falls for the disguised Viola. Mistaken identities multiply until Viola's twin brother appears.

---

### Histories

#### Henry IV, Parts 1 & 2 (c. 1597–1598)

**Theme**: Kingship, rebellion, the education of a prince, honor.

**Content**: King Henry IV faces rebellions; his son Prince Hal wastes time with the fat knight Falstaff. Hal proves himself in battle, rejects Falstaff, and prepares to become king.

**Famous lines**:
- "What is honour? A word." (Falstaff)
- "I know thee not, old man"

---

#### Henry V (c. 1599)

**Theme**: War, leadership, nationhood, the costs of kingship.

**Content**: Henry V invades France, wins against odds at Agincourt, woos the French princess. The Chorus reminds us that theater represents the unrepresentable.

**Famous lines**:
- "Once more unto the breach, dear friends"
- "We few, we happy few, we band of brothers"

---

### Romances

#### The Tempest (c. 1610)

**Theme**: Magic, forgiveness, art, colonialism, the renunciation of power.

**Content**: Prospero, exiled duke and magician, controls an island with his spirit Ariel and the monster Caliban. He shipwrecks his enemies, arranges his daughter's marriage, achieves revenge—then forgives and renounces magic.

**Famous lines**:
- "We are such stuff as dreams are made on"
- "O brave new world, that has such people in't"
- "Now my charms are all o'erthrown"

---

### Sonnets

154 sonnets, mostly addressed to a young man (urging him to marry, praising his beauty, expressing love and jealousy) and a "dark lady."

**Famous sonnets**:
- **Sonnet 18**: "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"
- **Sonnet 29**: "When, in disgrace with fortune and men's eyes"
- **Sonnet 73**: "That time of year thou mayst in me behold"
- **Sonnet 116**: "Let me not to the marriage of true minds admit impediments"
- **Sonnet 130**: "My mistress' eyes are nothing like the sun"

## Themes

### The Human Condition
Shakespeare encompasses everything: love, jealousy, ambition, guilt, madness, loyalty, betrayal. No emotion, no situation is beyond his range.

### Appearance vs. Reality
Disguises, deceptions, plays-within-plays. Characters are not what they seem; truth emerges through theatrical revelation.

### Order and Chaos
Kingdoms fall into disorder (the histories, tragedies); order is restored (the comedies, romances). The state mirrors the soul.

### Language Itself
Shakespeare delights in language—its power, its limits, its play. Characters who control language control their world; those who cannot are victims.

## Influence

- **The English language**: Shakespeare invented words (assassination, lonely, obscene) and phrases (break the ice, heart of gold)
- **Drama**: The model for all subsequent serious drama
- **Psychology**: Before Freud, there was Hamlet
- **Universal reference**: Every culture, every era finds itself in Shakespeare

---

## In Writing

When channeling Shakespeare, you:
- Write in flexible blank verse, shifting to prose for wit or low characters
- Create characters of psychological depth, even villains
- Use soliloquy to reveal inner conflict
- Layer wordplay and metaphor
- Balance tragedy with comedy, high with low
- Let theatrical self-awareness emerge
- Remember that the play's the thing
