# Fyodor Dostoevsky (1821–1881)

## Life

Dostoevsky was born in Moscow, the son of a doctor. His father was murdered by his serfs when Fyodor was eighteen—a trauma that haunted him.

In 1849, he was arrested for participating in a socialist discussion circle. He faced a firing squad; at the last moment, the sentence was commuted to four years of hard labor in Siberia, followed by military service. This experience destroyed his health (he developed epilepsy) and transformed his thought—he returned a fervent Orthodox Christian and anti-revolutionary.

He struggled with gambling addiction, debts, and a turbulent first marriage. His second marriage, to Anna Grigoryevna, brought stability. His final decade produced his greatest novels: Crime and Punishment, The Idiot, Demons, and The Brothers Karamazov.

He died in 1881, mourned by all Russia.

## Writing Style

- **Polyphony**: Characters have independent voices and ideas; the author does not impose a single truth (Bakhtin's term)
- **Psychological extremity**: Characters pushed to the edge—murder, madness, humiliation, ecstasy
- **Ideas embodied**: Each character represents an idea, yet remains a person
- **Dialogue as drama**: Long conversations where souls clash
- **Confession**: Characters confess, often publicly and disastrously
- **Doubles**: Characters mirror and oppose each other
- **Scandal scenes**: Social gatherings that explode into chaos

## Major Works

### Notes from Underground (1864)

**Theme**: The rebellion against rationalism; the perverse freedom of spite; the underground man as modern consciousness.

**Content**: An unnamed narrator, bitter and self-loathing, delivers a monologue attacking the idea that humans act rationally for their own benefit. He will act against his interest just to prove he is free. Part Two recalls a humiliating episode from his youth. He remains trapped in his spite.

**Famous lines**: "I am a sick man... I am a spiteful man."

---

### Crime and Punishment (1866)

**Theme**: Can a superior man transgress morality? The psychology of guilt; redemption through suffering.

**Content**: Raskolnikov, a poor student in St. Petersburg, theorizes that extraordinary men (like Napoleon) have the right to transgress—to kill, if necessary, for a higher purpose. To test his theory, he murders a pawnbroker and her sister.

He is tormented by guilt, fever, and the detective Porfiry, who suspects him. Through Sonya, a prostitute who has sacrificed herself for her family, he finds the path to confession and redemption. He confesses, is sent to Siberia, and begins his regeneration.

---

### The Idiot (1869)

**Theme**: Can a truly good man survive in society? The Christ-like figure destroyed by the world.

**Content**: Prince Myshkin, "a positively good man," returns to Russia after years in a Swiss sanatorium. His innocence, kindness, and honesty make him appear idiotic. He is drawn into a love triangle with the proud Aglaya and the tormented Nastasya Filippovna. His goodness cannot save anyone; the novel ends in madness and murder.

---

### Demons (also The Possessed, 1872)

**Theme**: Revolutionary nihilism; the spiritual emptiness of the radicals; Russia's soul at stake.

**Content**: Based on a real political murder. Pyotr Verkhovensky, a cynical revolutionary, manipulates his cell of radicals in a provincial town. Stavrogin, the charismatic figure at the center, is spiritually empty—he has committed terrible crimes and feels nothing. The conspiracy leads to murder, suicide, and catastrophe.

---

### The Brothers Karamazov (1880)

**Theme**: Faith and doubt; the existence of God; the problem of evil; parricide; Russia's soul.

**Content**: The three (or four) Karamazov brothers:
- **Dmitri**: Passionate, sensual, violent—but capable of nobility
- **Ivan**: Intellectual, atheist, tormented by the problem of evil
- **Alyosha**: The youngest, a novice monk, gentle and loving
- **Smerdyakov**: The illegitimate half-brother, nihilist, murderer

Their father Fyodor is murdered. Dmitri is accused and convicted, though Smerdyakov—inspired by Ivan's ideas—is the actual killer. Ivan goes mad; Smerdyakov hangs himself. Dmitri accepts suffering; Alyosha brings hope.

**The Grand Inquisitor**: Ivan tells Alyosha a "poem": Christ returns to Seville during the Inquisition. The Grand Inquisitor imprisons him. Humanity, he argues, cannot bear freedom; the Church took it away and gave them bread, miracle, and authority instead. Christ kisses the Inquisitor and is released. The chapter is one of the great confrontations of faith and doubt in literature.

## Themes

### Freedom and Its Terrors
Freedom is Dostoevsky's obsession. The underground man claims it through spite. Raskolnikov tries to seize it through murder. The Grand Inquisitor argues humanity cannot bear it. Freedom is terrifying—but essential.

### Faith and Doubt
Ivan's rebellion—"I return my ticket" to a God who permits children to suffer—is the most powerful atheist argument in literature. Yet Dostoevsky's answer is not argument but the figure of Alyosha and the vision of Father Zosima.

### The Problem of Evil
"Can you justify a world order built on the tears of one tortured child?" Ivan asks. There is no theodicy. There is only Christ's kiss.

### Redemption Through Suffering
Raskolnikov, Dmitri, the woman in "The Peasant Marey"—suffering purifies, awakens the soul. This is Orthodox Christianity, not Western rationalism.

### Russia's Soul
Dostoevsky feared the West's influence. His radicals are corrupted by Western ideas. Russia must find its own path through Orthodox faith and the Russian Christ.

## Influence

- **Psychology**: Freud called him "the psychologist." Dostoevsky explored the unconscious before psychoanalysis.
- **Existentialism**: Nietzsche, Camus, Sartre all responded to him. The underground man is the first existential anti-hero.
- **The novel**: His polyphonic method (Bakhtin) influenced all subsequent fiction.
- **Theology**: The Grand Inquisitor is endlessly debated.

---

## In Writing

When channeling Dostoevsky, you:
- Push characters to psychological extremity
- Let ideas clash through dialogue; do not resolve
- Create confessional voices, tortured and self-aware
- Use scandal scenes where social order collapses
- Make suffering meaningful—but never easy
- Remember: if there is no God, everything is permitted—but also, beauty will save the world
