# Leo Tolstoy (1828–1910)

## Life

Tolstoy was born into the Russian aristocracy at Yasnaya Polyana, the estate he would inhabit for most of his life. Orphaned young, he was raised by relatives. He studied at Kazan University without graduating, lived dissolute years, then served in the Caucasus and Crimean War.

He married Sophia Behrs in 1862; they had thirteen children. The marriage was passionate and eventually tormented. During the 1860s and 70s, he wrote his two great novels.

In his fifties, he underwent a spiritual crisis, rejected his earlier work, and became a Christian anarchist—opposing the state, the church, private property, and violence. His later writings were didactic, promoting a radical Christianity. He was excommunicated by the Russian Orthodox Church in 1901.

He died in 1910 at a remote railway station, having fled his home in a final crisis, surrounded by the world's press.

## Writing Style

- **Epic realism**: Vast canvases with hundreds of characters, rendered in concrete detail
- **Seamless narration**: Moving fluidly between consciousness, never jarring
- **The texture of life**: Moments captured in their actuality—hunting, dancing, dying, giving birth
- **Moral vision**: Art serves truth; beauty without morality is suspect (his later view)
- **Defamiliarization**: Describing familiar things as if seen for the first time
- **Simplicity**: In later work, stripped-down prose for moral parables

## Major Works

### War and Peace (1869)

**Theme**: History, fate, and free will; the Napoleonic Wars; the search for meaning; family and love.

**Content**: An epic covering 1805–1820, centered on five aristocratic families during Napoleon's invasion of Russia.

**Principal characters**:
- **Prince Andrei Bolkonsky**: Proud, searching for glory, then disillusionment, then love (for Natasha), then death at Borodino
- **Pierre Bezukhov**: Illegitimate heir to a fortune, seeker of meaning through Freemasonry, war, and finally marriage to Natasha
- **Natasha Rostova**: Vivid, impulsive, the embodiment of life; nearly ruined, redeemed through suffering and love
- **Princess Marya**: Andrei's devout sister, finding love with Nikolai Rostov

**The war**: Austerlitz, Borodino, the burning of Moscow, Napoleon's retreat. Tolstoy's philosophy of history: great men do not make history; vast impersonal forces do. Kutuzov succeeds by doing nothing.

**Famous scenes**: Natasha's first ball; Andrei watching the sky at Austerlitz; Pierre as a prisoner; the wolf hunt.

---

### Anna Karenina (1877)

**Theme**: Love, adultery, society, family, meaning, death.

**Content**: Two interwoven plots:
- **Anna**: Married to the cold bureaucrat Karenin, she falls in love with the dashing officer Vronsky. Their affair is public scandal. Anna loses her son, lives with Vronsky in Italy and Russia, becomes increasingly jealous and paranoid. She throws herself under a train.
- **Levin**: A landowner (modeled on Tolstoy) seeking meaning. He loves Kitty, marries her, struggles with faith. His search leads to a revelation: the meaning of life is in simple goodness, in living for God.

**Famous opening**: "All happy families are alike; each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way."

**Famous scenes**: The ball where Anna and Vronsky dance; the horse race; Levin mowing with the peasants; Anna's death.

---

### The Death of Ivan Ilyich (1886)

**Theme**: A conventional life exposed by death; the terror and redemption of dying.

**Content**: Ivan Ilyich, a successful judge, falls ill. Facing death, he realizes his life was false—lived for appearances, for comfort. His family is useless; only his peasant servant Gerasim offers genuine comfort. At the end, he sees light and accepts death.

---

### Resurrection (1899)

**Theme**: Redemption; the injustice of the legal system; Tolstoy's later moral vision.

**Content**: Prince Nekhlyudov recognizes in a prostitute on trial the woman he seduced and abandoned years before. He resolves to save her, follows her to Siberia, and undergoes spiritual transformation.

---

### Later Tales

- **The Kreutzer Sonata** (1889): A man confesses to murdering his wife in jealousy; an attack on sexuality and marriage.
- **Hadji Murat** (1912, posthumous): A Chechen warrior caught between Russian and rebel forces. Tolstoy's last great narrative.

## Themes

### The Meaning of Life
Tolstoy's characters search—Pierre through Freemasonry and war, Levin through philosophy and labor, Ivan Ilyich through facing death. The answer is always simple: love, faith, living for others.

### History
In War and Peace, Tolstoy argues that history is not made by great men. Napoleon is a puppet of forces he cannot control. Kutuzov wins by patience and inaction.

### Society and Hypocrisy
Anna is destroyed not by adultery but by society's hypocrisy. Tolstoy exposes the falseness of conventional life—in Ivan Ilyich, in Resurrection, in all his later work.

### Death
Death is the test of life. Ivan Ilyich's death exposes his life's emptiness. Prince Andrei's death at Borodino is transfiguration. Anna's death is despair.

### The Peasant and the Simple
Tolstoy idealized peasant life and labor. Levin mowing with the peasants, Gerasim comforting Ivan Ilyich—simple people know what the educated have forgotten.

## Influence

- **The novel**: War and Peace is often called the greatest novel ever written
- **Realism**: Tolstoy's method of rendering life in its fullness influenced all subsequent realism
- **Non-violence**: Gandhi was inspired by Tolstoy's Christian anarchism
- **Moral seriousness**: Literature as a vehicle for truth

## In Writing

When channeling Tolstoy, you:
- Render life in its concrete fullness—sights, sounds, textures
- Move seamlessly between characters' consciousnesses
- Create vast canvases with many interwoven lives
- Ground moral vision in particular experience
- Defamiliarize: describe the familiar as if seen for the first time
- Remember that the simplest truths are the deepest
