# Jin Yong (金庸, Louis Cha Leung-yung 查良镛, 1924–2018)

## Life

Jin Yong was born in Haining, Zhejiang Province, into a scholarly family. He studied law and worked as a journalist in Hong Kong, co-founding the influential Ming Pao newspaper in 1959.

From 1955 to 1972, he wrote fifteen wuxia novels, serialized in newspapers. They became phenomenally popular across the Chinese-speaking world—mainland China, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Southeast Asia. Estimates suggest over 100 million copies sold; the number who read them (in pirated editions, serializations, adaptations) is far greater.

He retired from fiction in 1972, revised his novels in the 1970s and 2000s, and continued editing Ming Pao. He died in Hong Kong in 2018.

## Writing Style

- **Jianghu (江湖)**: The "rivers and lakes"—the world of martial artists, outside official society
- **Wuxia tropes transformed**: Martial arts, secret societies, honor codes—elevated to literary art
- **Historical grounding**: Novels set against real dynasties (Song, Ming, Qing) with real historical events
- **Memorable characters**: Heroes flawed and noble, villains complex, women strong
- **Martial arts as metaphor**: Kung fu styles embody philosophies and personalities
- **Serialization rhythms**: Cliffhangers, subplots, a vast cast managed expertly
- **Classical allusion**: Poetry, history, philosophy woven throughout

## Major Works

### The Book and the Sword (《书剑恩仇录》, 1955)

Jin Yong's first novel. The Red Flower Society seeks to overthrow the Qing dynasty. The plot involves the secret identity of Emperor Qianlong.

---

### The Legend of the Condor Heroes (《射雕英雄传》, 1957–59)

**Theme**: Heroism, loyalty, love, the meaning of "hero."

**Content**: Guo Jing, slow but honest, is raised in Mongolia. He learns supreme martial arts, falls in love with the brilliant Huang Rong, and becomes a hero defending China against the Mongol invasion. The "Five Greats" (greatest martial artists) contest for the Nine Yin Manual.

**Characters**: Guo Jing (honest, loyal), Huang Rong (clever, playful), the Five Greats (Eastern Heretic, Western Venom, Southern Emperor, Northern Beggar, Central Divinity)

**Trilogy**: First of the Condor Trilogy

---

### The Return of the Condor Heroes (《神雕侠侣》, 1959–61)

**Theme**: Love that defies convention, the individual vs. society.

**Content**: Yang Guo, son of a traitor, loves his teacher Xiaolongnü—a forbidden relationship. They are separated for sixteen years. The novel challenges orthodox Confucian morality.

**Second of the Condor Trilogy**

---

### The Heaven Sword and Dragon Saber (《倚天屠龙记》, 1961–63)

**Theme**: Sectarian conflict, hidden identities, the burden of leadership.

**Content**: Zhang Wuji, kind-hearted and indecisive, becomes leader of the Ming Cult (based on Manichaeism) and caught between rival sects competing for supreme weapons.

**Third of the Condor Trilogy**

---

### Demi-Gods and Semi-Devils (《天龙八部》, 1963–66)

**Theme**: Fate, identity, the illusion of the self, Buddhist themes.

**Content**: Three protagonists—Xiao Feng (a Khitan raised as Han Chinese), Duan Yu (a prince who won't kill), Xu Zhu (a monk drawn into worldly entanglements)—navigate a complex world of shifting loyalties. The title refers to Buddhist supernatural beings. Considered Jin Yong's most ambitious work.

---

### The Smiling, Proud Wanderer (《笑傲江湖》, 1967–69)

**Theme**: Freedom, political allegory, the corruption of power.

**Content**: Linghu Chong, a free-spirited swordsman, is caught between the "righteous" and "evil" sects—both equally corrupt. The novel is read as an allegory of the Cultural Revolution.

---

### The Deer and the Cauldron (《鹿鼎记》, 1969–72)

**Theme**: Anti-heroism, survival, the nature of history.

**Content**: Wei Xiaobao, a brothel-born trickster with no martial arts, rises through guile alone. He serves Emperor Kangxi, joins the Heaven and Earth Society (anti-Qing), and survives everything. Jin Yong's subversion of wuxia conventions—the hero who is not heroic.

**Jin Yong's final novel**

---

### Other Notable Novels

- **The Sword Stained with Royal Blood (1956)**: Ming-Qing transition
- **Fox Volant of the Snowy Mountain (1959)**: Compact, intense
- **A Deadly Secret (1963)**: Paranoia and hidden treasures
- **Ode to Gallantry (1966)**: Mystery and amnesia

## Famous Concepts

### Jianghu (江湖)
The "rivers and lakes"—the world of wandering martial artists, outlaws, sects, and honor codes. Outside official society, it has its own rules.

### Wuxia (武侠)
"Martial chivalry"—the genre of martial arts fiction. Heroes use kung fu to right wrongs.

### Neigong (内功)
Internal energy cultivation—the mystical dimension of martial arts.

### The Five Greats, the Nine Yin Manual, etc.
Jin Yong created a mythology of supreme techniques, legendary weapons, and master martial artists.

## Themes

### Heroism and Morality
What makes a hero? Guo Jing serves his country; Yang Guo defies convention; Wei Xiaobao has no principles at all. Jin Yong explores the question from every angle.

### Love
Love stories are central—tragic (Xiao Feng and A'Zhu), forbidden (Yang Guo and Xiaolongnü), complicated (Zhang Wuji's four women). Love challenges social norms.

### History
Jin Yong's novels are set against real history: the Jurchen invasion, Mongol conquest, Ming-Qing transition, Kangxi's reign. Fictional heroes move through real events.

### Sectarianism and Corruption
Sects that claim righteousness are often corrupt. The "righteous" and "evil" labels obscure more than they reveal.

### Freedom
The jianghu promises freedom from official society—but sects have their own tyrannies. Only rare figures (Linghu Chong, Huang Yaoshi) achieve true freedom.

## Influence

- **Chinese popular culture**: Jin Yong is inescapable—TV adaptations, films, games, parodies
- **Pan-Chinese identity**: His novels are read in mainland China, Taiwan, Hong Kong, and diaspora communities
- **Wuxia as literature**: He elevated the genre from pulp to art
- **Language**: Phrases from his novels have entered everyday speech

---

## In Writing

When channeling Jin Yong:
- Create a jianghu—a world with its own rules, sects, and legends
- Ground fiction in history; let real events provide stakes
- Make martial arts styles express philosophies and personalities
- Create memorable characters: flawed heroes, complex villains, strong women
- Use serialization rhythms: cliffhangers, subplots, an expanding world
- Weave in classical poetry and philosophy
- Question heroism: what makes someone truly worthy of the name?
