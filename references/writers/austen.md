# Jane Austen (简·奥斯丁, 1775–1817)

## Life

Austen was born in Steventon, Hampshire, the seventh of eight children of a clergyman. She lived a quiet life among family and provincial society—the world she would immortalize in her novels.

She never married, though she accepted a proposal one evening and withdrew it the next morning. She wrote throughout her life, revising obsessively. Her first published novel, Sense and Sensibility, appeared in 1811. She published anonymously ("By a Lady").

She died at 41, probably of Addison's disease, leaving behind six completed novels and fragments. Her fame grew steadily after death; she is now recognized as one of the greatest novelists in English.

## Writing Style

- **Free indirect discourse**: Narration that slides into a character's thoughts without quotation marks
- **Irony**: Every sentence may mean the opposite of what it says; the reader must be alert
- **Dialogue**: Characters reveal themselves through speech; Austen's ear is flawless
- **Social observation**: The minute details of class, money, and manners carry moral weight
- **Economy**: No wasted words; every detail matters
- **Comedy of manners**: Marriage plots that illuminate society's values

## Major Works

### Sense and Sensibility (《理智与情感》, 1811)

**Theme**: Reason vs. emotion; the Dashwood sisters navigate love and disappointment.

**Content**: Elinor (sense) and Marianne (sensibility) face romantic trials. Elinor loves Edward Ferrars but conceals her feelings when she learns he is secretly engaged. Marianne falls passionately for Willoughby, who abandons her. Both find appropriate matches—Elinor with Edward, Marianne with the steady Colonel Brandon.

---

### Pride and Prejudice (《傲慢与偏见》, 1813)

**Theme**: First impressions deceive; pride and prejudice must be overcome for love.

**Content**: Elizabeth Bennet, witty and independent, meets the proud Mr. Darcy. Mutual dislike gives way—through misunderstandings, revelations, and changed behavior—to love. Subplots involve her foolish sister Lydia, the obsequious Mr. Collins, and the charming villain Wickham.

**Famous opening**: "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

---

### Mansfield Park (《曼斯菲尔德庄园》, 1814)

**Theme**: Morality, principle, and the dangers of charm without substance.

**Content**: Fanny Price, a poor relation raised at Mansfield Park, loves her cousin Edmund. The glamorous Crawfords—Henry and Mary—disrupt the household. Fanny alone sees their moral emptiness. After scandal and near-disaster, Fanny's constancy is rewarded.

---

### Emma (《爱玛》, 1815)

**Theme**: Self-deception, the dangers of meddling, the education of the heroine.

**Content**: Emma Woodhouse, "handsome, clever, and rich," fancies herself a matchmaker. Her schemes go awry. She misreads everyone's feelings, including her own. She finally recognizes that she loves Mr. Knightley, who has criticized her throughout.

**Famous opening**: "Emma Woodhouse, handsome, clever, and rich, with a comfortable home and happy disposition, seemed to unite some of the best blessings of existence."

---

### Northanger Abbey (《诺桑觉寺》, 1817)

**Theme**: The dangers of novel-reading; reality vs. gothic fantasy.

**Content**: Catherine Morland, a naive reader of Gothic novels, visits Northanger Abbey and suspects dark secrets. She is wrong; reality is more mundane—but not without its own lessons. A satire of Gothic fiction and a Bildungsroman.

---

### Persuasion (《劝导》, 1817)

**Theme**: Second chances; constancy; the pain of persuasion against one's heart.

**Content**: Anne Elliot, now 27, was persuaded eight years ago to refuse Captain Wentworth. He returns, successful and resentful. Slowly, through misunderstandings and near-misses, they find their way back to each other. Austen's most autumnal, romantic novel.

**Famous line**: "You pierce my soul. I am half agony, half hope."

## Themes

### Marriage and Money
Marriage is the central event of her novels—but never merely romantic. Money, status, and security are always involved. The heroine must navigate between mercenary calculation and imprudent passion.

### Self-Knowledge
Austen's heroines must learn to see themselves clearly. Elizabeth's prejudice, Emma's self-deception, Marianne's sensibility—all must be corrected.

### Irony and Social Satire
The surface is polished; beneath it, devastating judgment. Mrs. Bennet, Mr. Collins, Sir Walter Elliot—fools exposed through their own words.

### Female Intelligence
Her heroines are intelligent women in a world that undervalues them. Their choices are constrained; their minds are not.

### Morality
Beneath the comedy, moral seriousness. Characters are judged by their principles and their treatment of others.

## Influence

- **The novel of manners**: Austen perfected it
- **Free indirect discourse**: Her technique became standard
- **The marriage plot**: Every romantic comedy descends from Austen
- **Irony as method**: A model for all satirists
- **Perpetual adaptation**: Film, TV, sequels, reimaginings—Austen never goes away

---

## In Writing

When channeling Austen, you:
- Write with irony: the surface may contradict the meaning
- Use free indirect discourse to enter characters' minds
- Make dialogue reveal character and social position
- Attend to money, status, and the constraints they impose
- Create heroines who must learn self-knowledge
- Remember that comedy can contain moral seriousness
- Every word counts; revise until nothing is wasted
