# Du Fu (712–770)

## Life

Du Fu was born into a scholar-official family with a distinguished literary heritage. His grandfather Du Shenyan was a noted poet. Du Fu spent his youth studying for the civil service examinations, traveling, and writing poetry. He failed the examinations repeatedly.

In 755, the An Lushan Rebellion shattered the Tang dynasty's golden age. Du Fu experienced its horrors firsthand—separation from family, flight, poverty, the devastation of war. He briefly held minor posts but never achieved high office. He spent his later years wandering through Sichuan and the south, often ill and impoverished.

He died in 770, probably of illness, on a boat on the Xiang River.

During his lifetime, Du Fu was less celebrated than his friend Li Bai. Posterity reversed this: he became the "Poet-Sage" , the moral conscience of Chinese poetry.

## Writing Style

- **Realism**: Du Fu depicts what he sees—war's devastation, the people's suffering, his own hardship
- **Formal mastery**: Especially of the regulated verse form; precise tonal patterns, parallelism, compression
- **Moral weight**: Poetry as witness; the poet's duty to record and to care
- **Historical consciousness**: His poems are a chronicle of his age; "poem-history"
- **Range of register**: From elevated grandeur to colloquial intimacy
- **Compassion**: The suffering of ordinary people is his constant theme

## Major Works

### "A Song of War Chariots"

**Background**: Written around 750, before the An Lushan Rebellion, as the Tang empire waged costly campaigns.

**Content**: Soldiers march to endless war; families grieve; the dead are countless. "Do you not see the bones of the dead / Scattered along the shores of Kokonor? / The ghosts of the old and the newly dead cry out, / In the gray sky their wailing is heard through the rain."

**Theme**: War's human cost; the empire's overreach; the common people as victims.

---

### "Moonlit Night"

**Background**: Written in 756 after Du Fu was captured by rebels and separated from his family.

**Content**: The poet imagines his wife in Fuzhou, alone, watching the same moon. "Tonight from Fuzhou she watches the moon alone. / Far away, I feel sorry for our children, too young / To understand why she thinks of Chang'an."

**Theme**: Separation; longing; the moon as connector across distance.

---

### "Spring View"

**Background**: Written in 757 while Du Fu was held in rebel-occupied Chang'an.

**Content**: The capital is ruined; spring returns, but the state is broken. "The nation is destroyed; mountains and rivers remain. / In the city it is spring; grass and trees grow thick. / Grieving for the times, flowers draw tears; / Hating separation, birds alarm the heart."

**Theme**: The contrast between nature's renewal and human destruction; personal grief and national tragedy intertwined.

---

### "The Ballad of the Army Carts"

A long ballad on the endless conscription and the grief of those left behind.

---

### "Song of the Thatched Hut Damaged by the Autumn Wind"

**Background**: Written in 760 in Chengdu, where Du Fu built a modest thatched hut.

**Content**: Autumn wind tears off his roof; rain leaks in; his thin quilt is cold. Yet he concludes with a selfless wish: "If I could get a mansion of a million rooms, / To shelter all the poor scholars of the world, all with happy faces, / Unshaken by wind and rain, secure as a mountain— / Ah! When shall I see such a house before my eyes? / Then though my hut alone be destroyed and I freeze to death, I would be content."

**Theme**: Personal hardship transformed into compassion for all sufferers.

---

### "The Journey North"

**Background**: An autobiographical poem of 770 lines, recording Du Fu's journey to rejoin his family after the rebellion.

**Content**: The landscape of war; ruined villages; reunion with his starving family.

**Theme**: Personal narrative as national history.

---

### "Three Officials" and "Three Farewells"

**Background**: A group of six poems (759) depicting conscription and its devastation.

**Content**: Officials seizing men for the army; wives parting from husbands; old men and young boys taken because there are no more adult men.

**Theme**: War's destruction of ordinary life; the poet as witness.

---

### "Qiang Village" Trilogy

**Background**: Written when Du Fu finally returns to his family in 757.

**Content**: The shock of return; his family's disbelief that he lives; his children's tears; neighbors peering over the wall.

**Theme**: The personal within the historical; survival against odds.

---

### "Autumn Meditations"

**Background**: Eight linked regulated verses written in Kuizhou in 766.

**Content**: Memory, exile, the vanished glory of the capital, the poet's age and illness, the contrast between past splendor and present isolation.

**Theme**: Elegy for the Tang at its height; the poet's exile and mortality.

## Themes

### War and Its Victims
Du Fu lived through the An Lushan Rebellion (755–763), which killed millions. His poetry is the chronicle of this catastrophe: soldiers marching to death, conscription destroying families, cities in ruin.

### The Suffering of the Common People
Unlike much classical poetry focused on court and elite, Du Fu attends to peasants, soldiers, old women, children. His compassion is social and political.

### Exile and Homesickness
Du Fu spent most of his adult life wandering. The capital Chang'an, symbol of Tang glory, haunts his poetry as the lost home.

### Moral Witness
Poetry as ethical act. Du Fu's phrase "poem-history" captures his sense of the poet's duty to record.

### Aging and Mortality
His late poems are filled with illness, poverty, the sense of life running out. Yet he continues to write.

## Influence

- **The standard**: Du Fu became the model for serious Chinese poetry; the "Poet-Sage"
- **Poem-history**: Poetry as witness to historical events
- **Compassion as theme**: Attention to the suffering of ordinary people
- **Formal mastery**: His regulated verse set the standard for the form
- **Contrast with Li Bai**: Li Bai is the "Immortal" , romantic and transcendent; Du Fu is the "Sage" , moral and engaged

---

## In Writing

When channeling Du Fu, you:
- Witness suffering; do not look away
- Connect personal experience to historical events
- Write with formal precision, especially in structured verse
- Show compassion for ordinary people, not only heroes
- Use landscape to reflect inner states and historical conditions
- Balance grandeur with intimacy
- Let memory and loss inform your vision
- Remember that poetry is a moral act
