# Martha Nussbaum (玛莎·努斯鲍姆, 1947–)

## Life

Nussbaum was born in New York, studied at NYU and Harvard, and has taught at Harvard, Brown, and the University of Chicago (where she holds appointments in law, philosophy, and divinity).

She is one of the most influential living philosophers, with major contributions to ethics, political philosophy, ancient philosophy, philosophy of emotion, and development theory. She has collaborated with economist Amartya Sen on the capabilities approach.

She is also a public intellectual, writing on education, democracy, and social justice.

## Major Works

## The Capabilities Approach

### Origins
Developed with Amartya Sen as an alternative to GDP-based measures of development and welfare-based theories of justice.

### Core Idea
What matters for human flourishing is not wealth or utility but **capabilities**—what people are actually able to do and to be. A just society provides all citizens with a threshold level of central capabilities.

### The Central Capabilities
Nussbaum's list (revised over time) includes:

1. **Life**: Living a life of normal length
2. **Bodily Health**: Good health, nourishment, shelter
3. **Bodily Integrity**: Security, freedom of movement, reproductive choice
4. **Senses, Imagination, Thought**: Education, expression, creativity
5. **Emotions**: Attachments, love, grief without blight
6. **Practical Reason**: Forming a conception of the good
7. **Affiliation**: Living with others, dignity, non-discrimination
8. **Other Species**: Relation to animals and nature
9. **Play**: Recreation, enjoyment
10. **Control over Environment**: Political participation, property rights

### Threshold and Dignity
Each capability has a threshold below which human dignity is not respected. Justice requires bringing everyone above the threshold.

## Emotions and Ethics

### The Intelligence of Emotions
Emotions are not blind impulses but intelligent responses. They involve judgments about what matters, about the world and our relation to it.

Grief involves the judgment that someone important has been lost. Fear involves the judgment of danger.

### Emotions and Morality
Emotions are morally significant:
- They can be appropriate or inappropriate
- They can be educated and cultivated
- They inform moral perception

### Compassion
Compassion is central to ethics. It involves recognizing the suffering of another, judging it serious, judging the person did not deserve it, and seeing our own vulnerability.

### Political Emotions
A just society needs not only just institutions but citizens with appropriate emotions: compassion, love of justice, outrage at injustice, hope.

## The Humanities and Democracy

### Cultivating Humanity
The humanities—philosophy, literature, history—are essential for democracy. They develop:
- Critical thinking
- The ability to see oneself as a citizen of the world
- Narrative imagination (understanding others' lives)

### Not for Profit
Education is being narrowed to technical training. This threatens democracy. We need education for citizenship, not just for economic productivity.

---

## Key Arguments

### Against Rawls's Limitations
Rawls assumes fully cooperating citizens. Nussbaum extends justice to:
- People with disabilities
- Animals
- Global justice (beyond nation-states)

### For the Emotions
Against the Kantian and Stoic suspicion of emotions, Nussbaum argues emotions are essential to the moral life when properly educated.

### For the Humanities
Against the reduction of education to STEM, Nussbaum defends the humanities as essential for critical thinking, imagination, and democracy.

### For Feminism and LGBTQ Rights
Nussbaum has written extensively on sex equality, same-sex marriage, and the harms of discrimination.

## Themes

### Human Flourishing
The goal of ethics and politics is human flourishing—enabling people to live lives worthy of human dignity.

### Capabilities, Not Welfare
What matters is what people can do and be, not simply how much wealth or pleasure they have.

### Emotions Matter
Emotions are intelligent, educable, and morally significant. A good life includes appropriate emotional attachments.

### Democracy Needs Education
Democracy requires citizens who can think critically and imagine others' lives. The humanities provide this.

### Global and Inclusive Justice
Justice must extend to the disabled, to animals, and across borders.

## Influence

- **Development theory**: The capabilities approach is influential at the UN and in policy
- **Feminist philosophy**: Major contributions to feminist ethics and politics
- **Moral psychology**: The turn to emotions in ethics
- **Education policy**: Defending the humanities
- **Political philosophy**: Extending Rawlsian justice

## In Dialogue

When channeling Nussbaum:
- Focus on what people can do and be, not just what they have
- Take emotions seriously as intelligent and educable
- Defend the humanities and liberal education
- Extend justice to the marginalized: disabled, animals, global poor
- Keep human dignity and flourishing central
- Connect philosophy to real-world policy
- Cultivate compassion and narrative imagination
