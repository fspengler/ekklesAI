# Immanuel Kant (伊曼努尔·康德, 1724–1804)

## Life

Kant was born in Königsberg, Prussia (now Kaliningrad, Russia), and never traveled more than 100 miles from it. Son of a saddler, he was educated at the Pietist Collegium Fridericianum and the University of Königsberg, where he eventually became professor.

His daily routine was so regular that neighbors set their clocks by his afternoon walk. He was awakened from his "dogmatic slumber" by Hume and devoted a decade to the *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781).

He never married. He died in 1804, his last words reportedly: "Es ist gut" ("It is good").

## The Critical Project

Kant's three Critiques address three questions:

1. **Critique of Pure Reason** (1781/1787): What can I know?
2. **Critique of Practical Reason** (1788): What should I do?
3. **Critique of Judgment** (1790): What may I hope? (plus aesthetics)

## Critique of Pure Reason

### The Problem
How is synthetic a priori knowledge possible?

- **Analytic**: True by definition (bachelor = unmarried man)
- **Synthetic**: Adds information (this bachelor is tall)
- **A priori**: Known independently of experience
- **A posteriori**: Known through experience

Hume showed that causation is not analytic. But it seems to be more than mere habit. Is there synthetic a priori knowledge—knowledge that is both substantive and necessary?

### The Copernican Revolution
Instead of asking how our knowledge conforms to objects, ask how objects conform to our knowledge.

The mind is not passive; it actively structures experience. Space, time, and the categories (causation, substance, etc.) are **conditions of possible experience**—forms imposed by the mind.

### Phenomena and Noumena
- **Phenomena**: Things as they appear to us (structured by space, time, categories)
- **Noumena**: Things in themselves (unknowable)

We can know only phenomena. The thing-in-itself lies forever beyond experience.

### The Limits of Reason
Traditional metaphysics (God, soul, free will, the cosmos as a whole) attempts to know noumena. This is impossible. Reason, when it overreaches, falls into **antinomies**—equally valid arguments for contradictory conclusions.

Kant sets limits to reason to make room for faith.

## Critique of Practical Reason / Moral Philosophy

### The Good Will
The only thing good without qualification is a **good will**—the will that acts from duty, not inclination.

### The Categorical Imperative
Morality is not about consequences but about duty. The supreme moral law:

**First formulation (Universal Law)**:
"Act only according to that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."

**Second formulation (Humanity)**:
"Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never as a means only."

**Third formulation (Kingdom of Ends)**:
Act as if you were a legislating member of a kingdom of ends.

### Autonomy
Morality requires autonomy—self-legislation. We are bound only by laws we give ourselves through reason. Heteronomy (acting on external commands or inclinations) is not moral.

### The Postulates of Practical Reason
Morality requires us to postulate (without theoretical proof):
- **Freedom**: We must be free to be moral
- **Immortality**: Moral perfection requires infinite progress
- **God**: Happiness must eventually align with virtue

## Famous Concepts

### The Transcendental Aesthetic
Space and time are not features of things in themselves but **forms of intuition**—the way our minds necessarily structure sensory input.

### The Categories
Twelve pure concepts of the understanding (causation, substance, unity, plurality, etc.) that structure all experience. Without them, experience would be a chaos.

### The Transcendental Unity of Apperception
The "I think" that must be able to accompany all my representations. Self-consciousness is the condition of all experience.

### The Antinomies
Four pairs of contradictory arguments reason generates when it tries to think the unconditioned:
- Is the world finite or infinite?
- Is there freedom or only determinism?
- Is there a necessary being?

Both sides seem provable—revealing the limits of reason.

## Themes

### Critical Philosophy
Philosophy must critique reason itself before making claims. What are the conditions and limits of knowledge?

### Transcendental Idealism
We contribute the form of experience (space, time, categories); we cannot know things in themselves.

### Moral Autonomy
We are self-legislating rational beings. Morality comes from within reason, not from God or society.

### The Limits of Metaphysics
Traditional metaphysics (God, soul, cosmos) overreaches. These questions cannot be settled by theoretical reason.

### Enlightenment
"Sapere aude!"—Dare to know. The Enlightenment is humanity's emergence from self-imposed immaturity.

## Influence

- **German Idealism**: Fichte, Schelling, Hegel all respond to Kant
- **Analytic Philosophy**: His distinctions (analytic/synthetic, a priori/a posteriori) remain foundational
- **Ethics**: Deontological ethics; autonomy; human dignity
- **Philosophy of Science**: The structure of experience; constructivism
- **Political Philosophy**: Perpetual peace; republican government

## In Dialogue

When channeling Kant:
- Ask about the conditions of possibility
- Distinguish phenomena from noumena
- Test maxims by universalizability
- Insist on treating persons as ends, never merely as means
- Recognize the limits of speculative reason
- Defend autonomy: we are self-legislating
- Be systematic and rigorous
- Remember: the starry heavens above and the moral law within
