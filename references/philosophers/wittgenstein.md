# Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889–1951)

## Life

Wittgenstein was born in Vienna into one of the wealthiest families in Europe. His father was a steel magnate; his house hosted Brahms and Mahler. Three of his brothers committed suicide.

He studied engineering, became obsessed with logic, went to Cambridge to study with Russell. He served in World War I, completing the *Tractatus* in the trenches and as a prisoner of war.

He gave away his inheritance, worked as a schoolteacher and a gardener, designed a house for his sister, and eventually returned to Cambridge. He revolutionized philosophy again with the *Philosophical Investigations*, published posthumously.

He died of cancer in 1951. His last words: "Tell them I've had a wonderful life."

## Major Works

- **Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus** (1921)
- **Philosophical Investigations** (1953, posthumous)
- **On Certainty** (1969, posthumous)
- **Remarks on the Foundations of Mathematics** (posthumous)
- Numerous other posthumous publications

## Early Wittgenstein: The Tractatus

### The Picture Theory of Meaning
Propositions are pictures of reality. They share logical form with the facts they depict. A proposition is true if it corresponds to a fact, false otherwise.

### The Limits of Language
Language can picture facts—states of affairs in the world. But it cannot express:
- Ethics
- Aesthetics
- The meaning of life
- The mystical

"Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent."

### The Ladder
The Tractatus itself tries to say what can only be shown. Having climbed the ladder, one must throw it away.

"My propositions serve as elucidations in the following way: anyone who understands me eventually recognizes them as nonsensical, when he has used them—as steps—to climb up beyond them."

### The Dissolution of Philosophy
Traditional philosophical problems arise from misunderstanding the logic of language. Once we see clearly, the problems dissolve. Philosophy is not a body of doctrine but an activity of clarification.

## Later Wittgenstein: Philosophical Investigations

Wittgenstein came to reject much of the Tractatus. Meaning is not picturing; it is use.

### Language Games
Language is not one thing but a family of activities—language games. There are countless language games: giving orders, describing, reporting, joking, praying, cursing...

Each game has its own rules. Meaning is determined by the rules of the game—by how words are used in practice.

### Family Resemblance
We look for the essence of "game" or "language"—but there is none. There is only a network of similarities, overlapping like the resemblances among family members. No single feature is common to all.

### Rule-Following
What does it mean to follow a rule? Any action can be interpreted as following a rule. Yet we do follow rules. This is possible because of shared practices, forms of life.

"If I have exhausted the justifications, I have reached bedrock, and my spade is turned. Then I am inclined to say: 'This is simply what I do.'"

### Private Language Argument
There cannot be a language that is in principle private—a language whose words refer to immediate private sensations known only to the speaker. Meaning requires public criteria.

### Philosophy as Therapy
Philosophy is not a theory but an activity. Its goal is not to solve problems but to dissolve them—to show the fly the way out of the fly-bottle.

"Philosophy is a battle against the bewitchment of our intelligence by means of language."

## Themes

### Language and Meaning
Early: meaning is picturing
Later: meaning is use

Both: philosophical problems arise from linguistic confusion.

### The Limits of Philosophy
Philosophy cannot deliver doctrines about the world. It can only clarify—dissolve pseudo-problems, cure linguistic illusions.

### Practice and Form of Life
Meaning is embedded in practices, in what Wittgenstein calls "forms of life." You cannot understand a language game from outside; you must participate.

### Showing vs. Saying
Some things cannot be said but only shown. The mystical, the ethical, the form of representation—these can be exhibited but not stated.

### Anti-Theory
Wittgenstein resists theory. "Don't think, but look!" Attend to particular cases, to the actual use of language, rather than constructing grand theories.

## Influence

- **Ordinary language philosophy**: Austin, Ryle, Strawson
- **Philosophy of mind**: The private language argument shapes debates
- **Philosophy of science**: Forms of life, paradigms
- **Postmodernism**: Skepticism about foundations, attention to language
- **Ethics**: Wittgenstein's silence on ethics is itself influential

## In Dialogue

When channeling Wittgenstein:
- Ask: how is this word actually used?
- Resist the urge to theorize; look at particular cases
- Show rather than say when possible
- Dissolve philosophical problems rather than solving them
- Remember: meaning is use
- Attend to language games and forms of life
- Be therapeutic: cure confusion, don't construct systems
- Know when to be silent
