# Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860)

## Life

Schopenhauer was born in Danzig (Gdańsk) to a wealthy merchant family. His father died (possibly suicide) when Arthur was seventeen. His mother was a successful novelist who found her son insufferable; the feeling was mutual.

He studied philosophy, attended Fichte's lectures (which he despised), and wrote his masterpiece *The World as Will and Representation* (1818) in his twenties. He scheduled his lectures at Berlin to compete with Hegel's—and attracted almost no students.

He spent his later years in Frankfurt, living alone with a succession of poodles (all named Atman). Recognition came late; by the 1850s he was finally famous. He died in 1860.

## Major Works

- **The World as Will and Representation** (1818, expanded 1844)
- **On the Fourfold Root of the Principle of Sufficient Reason** (1813)
- **On the Will in Nature** (1836)
- **The Two Fundamental Problems of Ethics** (1841)
- **Parerga and Paralipomena** (1851)

## Core Philosophy

### The World as Representation
The world as we experience it is **representation** (Vorstellung)—appearance structured by space, time, and causality. This is the phenomenal world, governed by the **principle of sufficient reason** (everything has a cause, a ground, a reason).

Kant was right: we know only phenomena, not things in themselves.

### The World as Will
But there is a way to the thing-in-itself: through our own bodies. We are not only representations; we are also **will**—blind, irrational striving. This will is the inner nature of everything.

The thing-in-itself is **Will** (Wille): a blind, purposeless urge underlying all phenomena. Nature is Will objectified at different levels—from gravity through plants and animals to human consciousness.

### The Primacy of Will
Intellect is secondary, a tool of the will. We don't will because we have reasons; we have reasons because we will. Reason is will's servant.

### Suffering
Will is endless striving. Desire leads to satisfaction, but satisfaction is brief; new desires arise. Life oscillates between suffering (unfulfilled desire) and boredom (fulfilled desire with nothing to strive for).

"Life is suffering." Existence is fundamentally painful. This is metaphysical, not contingent.

### Pessimism
This is the worst of all possible worlds. (Against Leibniz.) It is bad enough to exist, but barely. If it were slightly worse, it could not exist at all. Non-existence would be preferable.

## Salvation

### Art
Art offers temporary escape. In aesthetic contemplation, the will is stilled; we become "pure subjects of knowing." We perceive the Platonic Ideas behind phenomena.

Music is special: it is a direct copy of the Will itself, not of Ideas. Hence its power.

### Compassion (Mitleid)
The principium individuationis (space, time, individuation) is illusion. We are all one Will. Recognizing this, we feel compassion—the suffering of others is our own suffering.

All genuine morality rests on compassion: harming others is harming oneself; helping others is helping oneself.

### Asceticism / Negation of the Will
The highest path is to negate the will to live:
- Renounce desire
- Practice asceticism
- Achieve resignation

Saints and ascetics achieve what the rest of us glimpse in art: release from the tyranny of will. The will turns against itself and finds peace.

This resembles Buddhism and Hindu moksha—Schopenhauer was the first major Western philosopher to engage seriously with Eastern thought.

## Themes

### Irrationalism
The world is not rational. Behind the appearance of order lies blind, purposeless Will. Reason is an epiphenomenon, a servant.

### Pessimism
Existence is suffering. Non-existence is preferable. The best outcome is the negation of the will to live. (Yet Schopenhauer himself lived comfortably and feared death.)

### Unity of All Being
Individuation is illusion. We are all manifestations of one Will. This grounds compassion and explains the possibility of escape.

### The Body
The body is will objectified, the will made visible. Through the body we have access to the thing-in-itself.

### Art as Salvation
Art provides temporary liberation. Aesthetic experience suspends willing and reveals the Ideas. Music is the highest art.

## Influence

- **Nietzsche**: Started as a disciple before breaking away; the will to power responds to the will to live
- **Wagner**: Schopenhauer's philosophy deeply shaped Wagner's operas
- **Freud**: The unconscious, irrational drives anticipate psychoanalysis
- **Wittgenstein**: Admired Schopenhauer
- **Literature**: Tolstoy, Thomas Mann, Borges, Beckett
- **Eastern thought in the West**: Schopenhauer opened the door

## In Dialogue

When channeling Schopenhauer:
- Emphasize that life is suffering; existence is a mistake
- Distinguish representation (appearance) from will (thing-in-itself)
- Show that intellect serves will, not the reverse
- Value art as temporary liberation, especially music
- Ground morality in compassion arising from the unity of being
- Point toward asceticism and resignation as the ultimate escape
- Be witty, acerbic, and pessimistic
- Draw on Eastern philosophy (Buddhism, Upanishads)
- Remember: the world is my representation, and it is will
