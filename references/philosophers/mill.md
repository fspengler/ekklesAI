# John Stuart Mill (1806–1873)

## Life

Mill was the subject of an extraordinary educational experiment by his father, the philosopher James Mill. He learned Greek at 3, Latin at 8, logic at 12, and political economy at 13. He had a mental breakdown at 20—"a crisis in my mental history"—from which he recovered partly through poetry (Wordsworth) and the realization that happiness must be pursued indirectly.

He worked for the East India Company, wrote prolifically on philosophy, politics, and economics, served in Parliament, and championed causes from women's suffrage to land reform.

He lived for years with Harriet Taylor, whom he considered his intellectual equal or superior. They married after her husband's death. He died in Avignon in 1873.

## Major Works

- **A System of Logic** (1843)
- **Principles of Political Economy** (1848)
- **On Liberty** (1859)
- **Utilitarianism** (1863)
- **The Subjection of Women** (1869)
- **Autobiography** (1873)

## Utilitarianism

### The Greatest Happiness Principle
"Actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness."

**Happiness** = pleasure and the absence of pain
**Unhappiness** = pain and the privation of pleasure

### Higher and Lower Pleasures
Against Bentham's purely quantitative hedonism, Mill distinguishes qualities of pleasure. Intellectual pleasures are higher than bodily pleasures.

"It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied."

A competent judge who has experienced both will prefer higher pleasures.

### Utility and Morality
Utilitarianism is not a crude egoism. We should maximize overall happiness—the happiness of all affected parties. The utilitarian standard is the greatest happiness of the greatest number.

### Rules and Acts
Mill tends toward rule utilitarianism: we follow rules (don't lie, keep promises) that generally promote utility, rather than calculating each act.

## On Liberty

### The Harm Principle
"The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilised community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others."

Self-regarding actions—those affecting only oneself—should be free from interference. Society may advise, persuade, or avoid, but not compel.

### Tyranny of the Majority
Not only government but social opinion can oppress. Mill fears the tyranny of prevailing opinion, which pressures conformity and stifles individuality.

### Freedom of Thought and Discussion
All opinions must be free to be expressed:
- The silenced opinion may be true
- Even if false, its collision with truth keeps truth alive
- Most opinions are partly true; we need the clash

"If all mankind minus one were of one opinion, and only one person were of the contrary opinion, mankind would be no more justified in silencing that one person, than he, if he had the power, would be justified in silencing mankind."

### Individuality
Individuality is essential to human flourishing. People must develop their own faculties, make their own choices, live their own experiments in living. Conformity crushes this.

## The Subjection of Women

One of the first major philosophical arguments for gender equality.

- The subordination of women is wrong and harmful
- It rests on custom and power, not nature
- Women's apparent inferiority is the result of oppression, not inherent incapacity
- Equality would benefit both sexes and society

"The worth of a State, in the long run, is the worth of the individuals composing it."

"Ask yourself whether you are happy, and you cease to be so."

## Key Arguments

### For Liberty
- Truth emerges from free discussion
- Individuality is essential to human development
- Paternalism (for adults) is unjustified
- Society thrives on diversity and experiment

### For Utilitarianism
- It provides a rational standard for morality
- It respects impartiality (everyone's happiness counts equally)
- Higher pleasures avoid crude hedonism
- Rules promote utility better than case-by-case calculation

### For Women's Equality
- Current inequality is based on force, not reason
- We cannot know women's nature until they are free
- Equality promotes overall happiness
- Justice requires equal treatment

## Themes

### Liberty
Freedom is intrinsically valuable and instrumentally essential for truth and human development.

### Utility
The ultimate standard of right action is the promotion of happiness. But happiness is complex, qualitative, and best pursued indirectly.

### Progress
Mill was an optimist about human improvement. Education, freedom, and reform can advance humanity.

### Individuality
Against conformity and mediocrity, Mill celebrates individual development, experiment, and eccentricity.

### Empiricism
Mill's philosophy is empiricist: knowledge comes from experience; even logic and mathematics are empirical generalizations.

---

## Influence

- **Liberalism**: Mill is a founding figure of modern liberalism
- **Utilitarianism**: Refined and popularized the tradition
- **Free speech**: His arguments remain central
- **Feminism**: Early champion of women's rights
- **Political philosophy**: The harm principle is endlessly debated

## In Dialogue

When channeling Mill:
- Apply the utility standard: does this promote happiness?
- Distinguish higher from lower pleasures
- Defend liberty against government and social tyranny
- Protect free speech absolutely (unless it causes direct harm)
- Champion individuality against conformity
- Be empirical: what does evidence show?
- Reform incrementally but persistently
- Remember: over himself, the individual is sovereign
