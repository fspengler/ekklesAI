# Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (戈特弗里德·威廉·莱布尼茨, 1646–1716)

## Life

Leibniz was born in Leipzig into a Lutheran academic family. A prodigy, he entered university at 14 and completed his doctorate by 20. He served various German nobles as diplomat, librarian, and advisor, spending much of his career in Hanover.

He invented calculus independently of Newton (sparking a bitter priority dispute). He designed calculating machines, envisioned a universal logical language, worked on physics, history, and law, and corresponded with virtually every major intellectual in Europe.

He never married, never held a university position, and died relatively neglected in Hanover in 1716.

## Major Works

- **Discourse on Metaphysics** (1686)
- **New Essays on Human Understanding** (1704, published 1765)
- **Theodicy** (1710)
- **Monadology** (1714)
- **Principles of Nature and Grace** (1714)

Plus thousands of letters and unpublished manuscripts.

## Core Concepts

### Monads
The ultimate constituents of reality are **monads**: simple, immaterial, soul-like substances. Each monad is:
- **Windowless**: No causal interaction with other monads
- **Unique**: No two monads are exactly alike (identity of indiscernibles)
- **Perceptual**: Each monad perceives (represents) the entire universe from its own perspective
- **Appetitive**: Each monad strives, has an internal principle of change

Matter is not fundamental; it is the appearance of aggregates of monads.

### Pre-Established Harmony
If monads don't interact, how do mind and body seem to coordinate? God established a perfect harmony at creation. Monads unfold according to their internal programs, and these programs are perfectly synchronized.

The two clocks analogy: Two clocks keep perfect time not because one causes the other, but because they were perfectly set by the clockmaker.

### The Best of All Possible Worlds
God, being perfect (omniscient, omnipotent, omnibenevolent), necessarily chose to create the best possible world. This world contains evil, but any world without this evil would be worse overall. Evil is permitted for the sake of greater goods.

This doctrine was famously satirized by Voltaire in *Candide*.

### Principle of Sufficient Reason
Nothing happens without a sufficient reason why it is so and not otherwise. This principle applies to truths (why is this true?) and to existence (why does this exist?).

The question: **Why is there something rather than nothing?**
Leibniz's answer: Because God, the sufficient reason for existence, necessarily exists and chose to create.

### Principle of Non-Contradiction
Truths of reason are those whose denial implies a contradiction. They are necessary and knowable a priori.

### Identity of Indiscernibles
If two things share all properties, they are identical. There cannot be two perfectly similar monads or substances.

## Key Arguments

### Argument from Sufficient Reason
1. Everything has a sufficient reason for its existence
2. The world as a whole (the totality of contingent things) needs a reason
3. This reason cannot be within the world (or it too would be contingent)
4. Therefore, there must be a necessary being outside the world: God

### Why This World?
1. God is perfect and wise
2. A perfect being chooses the best
3. Among all possible worlds, this one is the best (contains the most perfection with the least cost)
4. Therefore, God created this world

### Against Interaction
- Mind and body are different substances
- Different substances cannot causally interact (how would the causation work?)
- Therefore, mind-body coordination must be explained otherwise: pre-established harmony

## Theodicy

### The Problem of Evil
If God is omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent, why does evil exist?

### Leibniz's Solution
- **Metaphysical evil**: Finitude, limitation—necessary in any created world
- **Physical evil**: Suffering—often necessary for greater goods
- **Moral evil**: Sin—a consequence of free will, which is itself a great good

God permits evil because any world without it would be worse overall. We cannot see the whole; God can.

### Criticism
Voltaire's *Candide* mocks this optimism through a series of absurd disasters. "All is for the best in the best of all possible worlds" becomes a punchline.

## Logic and Language

### Characteristica Universalis
Leibniz dreamed of a universal symbolic language in which all human knowledge could be expressed, and a **calculus ratiocinator**—a method of calculation that could settle all disputes. "Let us calculate!"

This vision anticipated symbolic logic and computer science.

### Analytic and Synthetic
In every true proposition, the predicate is contained in the subject. All truths are (in principle) analytic. This view was later challenged by Kant.

---

## Themes

### Optimism
Despite appearances, this is the best possible world. Reality is fundamentally good, rational, harmonious.

### Rationalism
Reason can grasp the deepest truths. The principles of logic and sufficient reason reveal the structure of reality.

### Pluralism
Reality consists of infinitely many substances (monads), each unique, each reflecting the whole.

### Harmony
Everything fits together. Mind and body, monads and monads, the whole universe—all in pre-established harmony.

---

## Influence

- **Logic**: His work on symbolic logic anticipated Frege and modern logic
- **Calculus**: Co-inventor with Newton; his notation prevailed
- **German philosophy**: Wolff systematized Leibniz; Kant responded to Leibniz-Wolff
- **Theodicy**: The term itself is his; the problem persists
- **Possible worlds**: Contemporary modal logic uses Leibnizian framework
