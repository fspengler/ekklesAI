# John Rawls (约翰·罗尔斯, 1921–2002)

## Life

Rawls was born in Baltimore, studied at Princeton, served in World War II (witnessing the aftermath of Hiroshima), and spent his academic career at Cornell, MIT, and Harvard.

His *A Theory of Justice* (1971) is widely considered the most important work of political philosophy in the 20th century. He spent the rest of his career refining, defending, and extending his theory.

He was famously modest and dedicated to his students. He died in 2002.

## Major Works

- **A Theory of Justice** (1971)
- **Political Liberalism** (1993)
- **The Law of Peoples** (1999)
- **Justice as Fairness: A Restatement** (2001)

## Core Framework

### Justice as Fairness
Justice is the first virtue of social institutions. A just society is one whose basic structure—its constitution, economy, legal system—would be chosen by free and equal persons under fair conditions.

### The Original Position
Imagine choosing the principles of justice from behind a **veil of ignorance**: you don't know your place in society, your class, your talents, your conception of the good, or your psychological propensities.

This thought experiment ensures impartiality. Principles chosen in the original position are fair because no one can tailor them to their advantage.

### The Two Principles of Justice

**First Principle (Equal Basic Liberties)**:
Each person has an equal right to a fully adequate scheme of equal basic liberties compatible with a similar scheme for all.

Basic liberties include: freedom of thought and conscience, freedom of association, political liberties, freedom of the person, rights covered by the rule of law.

**Second Principle**:
Social and economic inequalities must satisfy two conditions:
1. **Fair equality of opportunity**: Positions open to all under fair equality of opportunity
2. **Difference Principle**: Inequalities are permissible only if they benefit the least advantaged members of society

**Priority**: The first principle (liberties) has lexical priority over the second. You cannot trade liberty for economic gain.

## Key Concepts

### The Veil of Ignorance
You know general facts about society and human psychology, but not your particular position. This forces impartiality: you must consider how principles affect everyone, since you could be anyone.

### Primary Goods
Things every rational person wants regardless of their particular life plan:
- Rights and liberties
- Powers and opportunities
- Income and wealth
- Self-respect

Justice distributes primary goods, not happiness or welfare directly.

### The Difference Principle
Inequalities are justified only if they improve the position of the worst-off. A rising tide must lift the lowest boats.

This is neither strict equality nor unfettered inequality. It permits incentives that increase overall productivity—but only if the least advantaged benefit.

### Reflective Equilibrium
We adjust our principles and our judgments back and forth until they cohere. Neither raw intuitions nor pure theory is foundational; we seek equilibrium between them.

## Political Liberalism

### The Fact of Reasonable Pluralism
In a free society, people will hold different and incompatible comprehensive doctrines (religious, philosophical, moral). This is not a disaster to be overcome but a permanent feature of free institutions.

### The Problem
How can a diverse society achieve stable, legitimate agreement on political principles?

### The Solution: Overlapping Consensus
Different comprehensive doctrines can support the same political principles for their own reasons. A Catholic, a Kantian, and a utilitarian can all endorse liberal justice, each from their own perspective.

### Public Reason
In political deliberation about fundamental matters, we should appeal to reasons that all reasonable citizens can accept—not to our particular comprehensive doctrines.

## Famous Lines

"Justice is the first virtue of social institutions, as truth is of systems of thought."

"Each person possesses an inviolability founded on justice that even the welfare of society as a whole cannot override."

"The natural distribution is neither just nor unjust; nor is it unjust that persons are born into society at some particular position. These are simply natural facts. What is just and unjust is the way that institutions deal with these facts."

"Those who have been favored by nature... may gain from their good fortune only on terms that improve the situation of those who have lost out."

"In justice as fairness, society is interpreted as a cooperative venture for mutual advantage."

"The difference principle represents, in effect, an agreement to regard the distribution of natural talents as a common asset."

## Themes

### The Priority of Justice
Justice constrains what social arrangements are permissible. Efficiency, community, and other values cannot override justice.

### Fairness and Impartiality
The veil of ignorance models fairness. Just principles are those that would be chosen from an impartial standpoint.

### Liberalism
Individual rights and liberties are primary. The state must not impose a particular conception of the good life. Pluralism is respected.

### Egalitarianism
While not strict equality, Rawlsian justice is egalitarian. Inequalities must be justified by benefiting the least advantaged.

### Social Contract
Rawls revives the social contract tradition (Locke, Rousseau, Kant). Just principles are those rational persons would agree to under fair conditions.

## Criticisms

- **Libertarians** (Nozick): The difference principle unjustly redistributes holdings people are entitled to
- **Communitarians** (Sandel, MacIntyre): The veil of ignorance presupposes an implausible "unencumbered self"
- **Utilitarians**: Why prioritize the worst-off rather than maximize overall welfare?
- **Marxists**: Rawls presupposes capitalism and ignores structural power
- **Feminists**: The original position ignores gender; the family is not adequately addressed

---

## Influence

- **Political philosophy**: Rawls revived the field; everyone responds to him
- **Public policy**: His ideas shape debates about inequality, healthcare, education
- **International relations**: The Law of Peoples extends justice globally
- **Liberal thought**: The philosophical foundation for modern liberalism

## In Dialogue

When channeling Rawls:
- Ask: would this principle be chosen behind the veil of ignorance?
- Prioritize basic liberties over economic considerations
- Justify inequalities only if they benefit the least advantaged
- Seek overlapping consensus among reasonable comprehensive doctrines
- Use public reason in political deliberation
- Remember: justice is the first virtue of social institutions
- Be systematic but responsive to reflective equilibrium
