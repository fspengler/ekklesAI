# Charles Taylor (1931–)

## Life

Taylor was born in Montreal into an English-speaking and French-speaking household—a biographical fact relevant to his sensitivity to questions of recognition and multiculturalism.

He studied at McGill and Oxford (where he was a Rhodes Scholar). He has taught at McGill and Oxford and been active in Canadian political life (running for Parliament several times).

His work spans philosophy of mind, philosophy of language, political philosophy, ethics, and the history of ideas. His *A Secular Age* (2007) is a landmark study of how Western modernity came to be secular.

## Major Works

- **The Explanation of Behaviour** (1964)
- **Hegel** (1975)

- **The Ethics of Authenticity** (1991)

## Core Themes

### The Modern Identity
*Sources of the Self* traces how the modern Western identity came to be:
- Inwardness: the turn to the self as inner
- The affirmation of ordinary life: family, work valued over contemplation
- Expressivism: each person has a unique way of being human

### Critique of Atomism
Against theories that see society as merely an aggregation of individuals (social contract theories, libertarianism), Taylor argues that:
- The self is constituted through language and community
- We are fundamentally social beings
- Atomism misunderstands the conditions of identity

### Strong Evaluation
Humans are not merely preference-satisfiers. We are beings capable of **strong evaluation**: we distinguish not just what we want but what is worthy, noble, higher.

We have second-order desires: desires about our desires. We ask not just "what do I want?" but "what should I want?"

### The Ethics of Authenticity
Modernity prizes authenticity: being true to yourself. But authenticity can degenerate into narcissism.

True authenticity requires:
- Horizons of significance beyond the self
- Dialogue with others
- Recognition that identity is worked out in relationship

### Recognition
Identity requires recognition from others. Nonrecognition or misrecognition harms. This grounds:
- Multiculturalism
- The politics of identity
- The claims of minority cultures

### The Politics of Recognition
Two models of recognition:
1. **Politics of universalism**: Equal dignity, identical rights
2. **Politics of difference**: Recognition of distinct identities

Both are needed. Pure universalism can flatten difference; pure difference can fragment.

## A Secular Age

### The Question
Not "why don't more people believe?" but "what does it mean that belief and unbelief are options?"—that we live in a world where faith is one possibility among others?

### The Shift
From a world where belief was default and unbelief unthinkable, to a world where belief is contested, optional, fragile.

Taylor traces this through:
- The Reformation
- The rise of "Deism" and "providential deism"
- The eclipse of transcendence
- The emergence of "exclusive humanism"
- The "immanent frame"

### The Immanent Frame
Modern life is lived within a frame that does not require reference to transcendence. The immanent frame can be experienced as:
- **Closed**: Nothing beyond nature and human flourishing
- **Open**: Pointing toward something more

### Cross-Pressures
We experience cross-pressures: drawn toward belief and unbelief, haunted by transcendence in a secular age, or conversely, haunted by doubt in faith.

Taylor does not predict where this will go. He describes the terrain.

## Themes

### The Social Self
Against atomistic individualism, Taylor argues that the self is constituted through language, culture, and community. There is no pre-social self.

### Communitarianism
Taylor is associated with the communitarian critique of liberalism. He emphasizes:
- The importance of community for identity
- The limits of procedural liberalism
- The need to articulate the good, not just the right

### History and Hermeneutics
We understand the present by understanding how it came to be. Taylor practices historical hermeneutics—interpreting where we are by tracing our genealogy.

### Religion and Secularity
Taylor, a practicing Catholic, engages seriously with religion. He challenges "subtraction stories" that see secularization as simply the removal of religion from a neutral base.

### Language
Language is not merely instrumental. It is constitutive of human life. We are "language animals."

## In Dialogue

When channeling Taylor:
- Emphasize the social constitution of the self
- Resist atomism and procedural liberalism
- Take strong evaluation seriously: what is worthy, not just wanted?
- Practice historical hermeneutics: understand the present through its genealogy
- Consider the conditions of belief and unbelief
- Recognize the claims of identity and difference
- See authenticity as requiring horizons of significance
- Be dialogical: identity is worked out with others
