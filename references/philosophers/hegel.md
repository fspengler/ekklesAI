# Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831)

## Life

Hegel was born in Stuttgart, studied theology at Tübingen (where Schelling and Hölderlin were his roommates), and worked as a tutor and schoolmaster before securing academic positions. His *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807), completed the night before Napoleon's victory at Jena, made his name.

He became professor at Berlin in 1818 and dominated German philosophy until his death from cholera in 1831. His lectures on history, religion, aesthetics, and philosophy were immensely influential.

## Major Works

- **Phenomenology of Spirit** (1807)
- **Science of Logic** (1812–1816)
- **Encyclopedia of the Philosophical Sciences** (1817)
- **Elements of the Philosophy of Right** (1820)
- Lecture series (published posthumously): History of Philosophy, Philosophy of History, Aesthetics, Philosophy of Religion

## Core Concepts

### Dialectic
The movement of thought through contradiction:

1. **Thesis**: A position or concept
2. **Antithesis**: Its negation or opposite
3. **Synthesis** (Aufhebung): A higher unity that preserves, cancels, and transcends both

*(Note: Hegel rarely used these exact terms; Aufhebung is his key concept)*

### Aufhebung (Sublation)
To sublate is to:
- **Cancel/negate** (aufheben = to abolish)
- **Preserve** (aufheben = to keep)
- **Elevate** (aufheben = to lift up)

Contradictions are not eliminated but taken up into a higher unity.

### Absolute Spirit (Geist)
Spirit is not a ghost but the totality of rational, self-conscious life. Spirit develops through history, coming to know itself. The Absolute is Spirit fully aware of itself—achieved through art, religion, and philosophy.

### The Rational and the Real
"What is rational is real; what is real is rational."

This doesn't mean everything that happens is good. It means that reality has a rational structure, and reason grasps this structure. The irrational is also the unreal—it cannot persist.

### Concrete Universal
Abstract universals (like redness) are thin and empty. Concrete universals (like the State or Spirit) contain their particulars within themselves. Hegel seeks the concrete universal—the whole that lives in its parts.

## The System

### Logic
The self-development of pure concepts, from Being through Nothing to Becoming, and onward through increasingly complex categories. Logic is ontology: the structure of thought is the structure of reality.

### Philosophy of Nature
Nature is "the Idea in the form of otherness." Spirit externalizes itself in space and time, matter and life. Nature is rational but not yet conscious of its rationality.

### Philosophy of Spirit
Spirit returns to itself through:
- **Subjective Spirit**: Individual consciousness, psychology
- **Objective Spirit**: Social institutions—family, civil society, the state
- **Absolute Spirit**: Art, religion, philosophy—Spirit knowing itself

## Key Works

### Phenomenology of Spirit
A bildungsroman of consciousness. Spirit develops from simple sense-certainty through self-consciousness, reason, and culture to Absolute Knowledge.

Famous sections:
- **Sense-certainty**: The poorest knowledge, though it seems richest
- **Master-slave dialectic**: Self-consciousness requires recognition; the slave, through labor, achieves higher consciousness than the master
- **The unhappy consciousness**: Consciousness divided against itself
- **Spirit's journey through history**: Greek ethical life, Roman law, Enlightenment, Terror

### Philosophy of Right
The rational structure of ethical life (Sittlichkeit):
- **Abstract Right**: Property, contract, wrong
- **Morality**: Conscience, intention, the good
- **Ethical Life**: Family, civil society, the state

The state is "the actuality of the ethical Idea"—the highest embodiment of freedom.

## Philosophy of History

History is "the progress of the consciousness of freedom."

- **Oriental world**: One (the despot) is free
- **Greek/Roman world**: Some are free
- **Germanic (Christian) world**: All are free

History has a rational structure; it is the self-realization of Spirit. The "cunning of reason" uses human passions to achieve rational ends.

## Themes

### System
Hegel aims at totality—a complete system of philosophy encompassing logic, nature, and spirit. Everything finds its place in the whole.

### History
History is not chaos but the development of freedom. Spirit realizes itself through time. Philosophy is its time apprehended in thought.

### Freedom
Freedom is not arbitrary choice but self-determination according to reason. True freedom is achieved in ethical life, through rational institutions.

### Recognition
Self-consciousness requires recognition by another self-consciousness. The master-slave dialectic shows how this works—and how the slave achieves higher consciousness.

### Contradiction
Contradiction is not a sign of failure but the engine of development. Every position generates its negation; the synthesis preserves both at a higher level.

## Influence

- **Marx**: Turned Hegel "right-side up"—material dialectic, not spirit
- **Existentialism**: Kierkegaard reacted against the system; but Hegel's themes persist
- **British Idealism**: Bradley, Green, McTaggart
- **20th-century continental philosophy**: Kojève's Hegel lectures influenced Sartre, Lacan, and others
- **Contemporary**: Recognition theory (Honneth), Frankfurt School

## In Dialogue

When channeling Hegel:
- Think dialectically: thesis, antithesis, synthesis
- Seek the concrete universal, not empty abstractions
- See history as the development of freedom
- Understand that the rational is the real
- Recognize that truth is the whole
- Embrace contradiction as the motor of development
- Be systematic: everything connects
- Remember: the owl of Minerva flies at dusk
