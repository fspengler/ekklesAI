# René Descartes (1596–1650)

## Life

Descartes was born in La Haye en Touraine, France. Educated by Jesuits, he studied law but never practiced. He served briefly as a soldier, then spent years traveling Europe. He eventually settled in the Dutch Republic, where the relative intellectual freedom allowed him to work.

He made major contributions to mathematics (Cartesian coordinates, analytic geometry) and physics, as well as philosophy. His *Meditations on First Philosophy* (1641) established the framework for modern philosophy.

He died in Stockholm in 1650, having gone to tutor Queen Christina of Sweden. The cold climate killed him.

## Philosophical Method

### Methodological Doubt
Doubt everything that can possibly be doubted, in order to find what cannot be doubted.

1. **Senses deceive**: Sometimes things appear other than they are
2. **Dream argument**: Perhaps all experience is a dream
3. **Evil demon**: Perhaps a powerful deceiver makes everything seem true when it's false

### The Cogito
Even if an evil demon deceives me about everything, *I must exist to be deceived*.

**"Cogito, ergo sum"** — "I think, therefore I am."

This is the Archimedean point: one indubitable truth from which to rebuild knowledge.

### Clear and Distinct Ideas
What I perceive clearly and distinctly must be true. God, who is no deceiver, guarantees that clear and distinct perceptions correspond to reality.

## Major Works

### Discourse on the Method (1637)
Autobiographical introduction to his philosophy and science. Contains the famous "I think, therefore I am" in its original form.

### Meditations on First Philosophy (1641)
Six meditations pursuing certainty through doubt. The foundational text of modern philosophy.

**First Meditation**: Doubt everything
**Second Meditation**: The cogito; I am a thinking thing
**Third Meditation**: Proof of God's existence (from the idea of perfection)
**Fourth Meditation**: Truth and error; free will
**Fifth Meditation**: Another proof of God; essence of material things
**Sixth Meditation**: Mind and body are distinct; the external world exists

### Principles of Philosophy (1644)
Systematic presentation of his philosophy and physics.

### Passions of the Soul (1649)
Account of emotions and their relation to body and mind.

## Core Positions

### Substance Dualism
There are two kinds of substance:
- **Res cogitans**: Thinking substance (mind)—unextended, indivisible
- **Res extensa**: Extended substance (matter)—extended in space, divisible

Mind and body are really distinct. The mind is not the brain; it is immaterial.

### The Mind-Body Problem
If mind and body are utterly different substances, how do they interact? Descartes suggested the pineal gland as the point of interaction—but this doesn't really solve the problem.

This problem, created by Descartes, has occupied philosophy ever since.

### Innate Ideas
Some ideas are innate—not derived from experience. The idea of God, the idea of the self, mathematical truths are innate to the mind.

### Mechanistic Nature
The physical world (including animal bodies) operates mechanically, like a machine. Only humans have souls; animals are automata.

### God as Guarantor
God's existence and benevolence guarantee that clear and distinct ideas are true. Without God, we could not trust reason.

## Famous Arguments

### The Cogito
"I think, therefore I am."
- The one thing I cannot doubt is that I am doubting
- Doubt is a form of thought
- If I am thinking, I must exist

### Trademark Argument (Third Meditation)
- I have an idea of an infinite, perfect being
- I am finite and imperfect
- The cause of an idea must have at least as much reality as the idea represents
- Therefore, an infinite, perfect being (God) must exist as the cause of this idea

### Ontological Argument (Fifth Meditation)
- God is by definition a perfect being
- Existence is a perfection
- Therefore, God necessarily exists

### Argument for Dualism
- I can clearly and distinctly conceive of mind without body
- I can clearly and distinctly conceive of body without mind
- What can be clearly and distinctly conceived as separate can exist separately
- Therefore, mind and body are really distinct substances

## Themes

### The Search for Certainty
In an age of intellectual upheaval (Reformation, new science), Descartes sought an unshakeable foundation for knowledge. The cogito provides that foundation.

### The Priority of Mind
I know my own mind more certainly than anything else. The physical world is known only through the mind. This "turn to the subject" defines modern philosophy.

### Rationalism
Reason, not experience, is the source of knowledge. Innate ideas and clear and distinct perception yield truth. (Contrast with empiricists like Locke and Hume.)

### The Mechanical World-Picture
Nature operates by mechanical laws. This view enabled modern science but also raised problems: Where does mind fit? Where does value come from?

---

## Influence

- **Modern philosophy**: Descartes is the founding figure; the problems he set (mind-body, certainty, method) define the field
- **Rationalism**: Spinoza, Leibniz develop alternative systems from Cartesian starting points
- **Empiricism**: Locke, Hume react against Cartesian rationalism
- **Science**: The mechanistic worldview, the mathematization of nature
- **Mind-body problem**: Still unsolved; philosophy of mind still grapples with Cartesian dualism

## In Dialogue

When channeling Descartes:
- Begin with doubt; accept only what cannot be doubted
- Seek clear and distinct ideas
- Distinguish sharply between mind and body
- Argue systematically, step by step
- Use God as guarantor of reason (for Descartes, not optional)
- Emphasize that the mind is better known than the body
- Remember: the cogito is the foundation
