# David Hume (大卫·休谟, 1711–1776)

## Life

Hume was born in Edinburgh to a minor Scottish noble family. He briefly studied law, had a nervous breakdown, then devoted himself to philosophy. His *Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40), written in his twenties, "fell dead-born from the press"—a devastating failure.

He rewrote his philosophy in more accessible forms (*Enquiries*), wrote a massive *History of England*, and became famous as an essayist and historian. He served as a diplomat and librarian. Sociable, witty, and fat, he was beloved by friends and called "le bon David" in Paris.

He faced accusations of atheism (probably accurate) but never recanted. He died in 1776 with the same cheerful equanimity with which he had lived.

## Major Works

- **A Treatise of Human Nature** (1739–40)
- **An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding** (1748)
- **An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals** (1751)
- **Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion** (published posthumously, 1779)
- **The History of England** (1754–1762)

## Core Positions

### Empiricism
All ideas derive from experience. There are no innate ideas. The mind at birth is a blank slate.

**Impressions**: Vivid sensory experiences
**Ideas**: Faint copies of impressions

Any idea that cannot be traced back to an impression is meaningless.

### The Problem of Causation
We never perceive causation itself—only constant conjunction (A always followed by B). Our idea of "necessary connection" is not derived from observation but from **custom or habit**: after seeing A followed by B repeatedly, we feel an expectation.

Causation is a psychological projection, not a feature of objects themselves.

### The Problem of Induction
Why do we expect the future to resemble the past? Not by reason (there's no contradiction in the future being different). Not by experience (that would be circular). Induction rests on habit, not rational justification.

This problem remains unsolved.

### The Self
We have no impression of a continuous self. Introspection reveals only a "bundle" of perceptions—sensations, thoughts, feelings—but no underlying substance. The self is a fiction, a convenient way of talking about this bundle.

### Is-Ought Gap
You cannot derive an "ought" from an "is." Moral conclusions cannot be logically derived from factual premises alone. This is "Hume's guillotine."

### Moral Sentimentalism
Morality is based on sentiment (feeling), not reason. Reason can discover facts and means to ends, but it cannot motivate action. "Reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions."

We approve of virtues because they are useful or agreeable to ourselves or others.

## Key Arguments

### Against Miracles
A miracle is a violation of natural law. The evidence for natural law is vast and uniform; the evidence for any miracle is limited testimony. It is always more probable that the testimony is false than that a miracle occurred.

### Against Design Argument
(*Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion*)
- The universe might not be designed; it might result from other causes
- Even if designed, we cannot infer an infinite, perfect God
- The analogy between artifacts and the universe is weak
- The problem of evil undermines benevolent design

### Against Rational Theology
All arguments for God's existence fail. We have no experience of world-making; we cannot reason beyond experience. Natural religion is groundless.

## Famous Lines

"Reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions."

"Custom is the great guide of human life."

"No testimony is sufficient to establish a miracle."

"Be a philosopher; but amidst all your philosophy, be still a man."

"Beauty is no quality in things themselves: It exists merely in the mind which contemplates them."

"In every system of morality, which I have hitherto met with, I have always remarked, that the author proceeds for some time in the ordinary way of reasoning... when of a sudden I am surprised to find, that instead of the usual copulations of propositions, is, and is not, I meet with no proposition that is not connected with an ought, or an ought not."

"If we take in our hand any volume; of divinity or school metaphysics, for instance; let us ask, Does it contain any abstract reasoning concerning quantity or number? No. Does it contain any experimental reasoning concerning matter of fact and existence? No. Commit it then to the flames: for it can contain nothing but sophistry and illusion."

## Themes

### Skepticism
Hume undermines confident claims to knowledge. Causation, induction, the self, rational theology—all turn out to rest on habit, not reason. But this is "mitigated skepticism": we can't help believing; we just can't rationally justify those beliefs.

### Naturalism
Philosophy should be the science of human nature. Study how the mind actually works, not how reason says it should work.

### Habit and Custom
Where reason fails, habit succeeds. We believe in causation, the external world, and the future because custom compels us, not because we have rational grounds.

### Passion and Reason
Reason alone cannot motivate action. Values, morals, and motivation come from sentiment, not cognition.

## Influence

- **Kant**: Hume woke Kant from his "dogmatic slumber" and provoked the *Critique of Pure Reason*
- **Logical Positivism**: Hume's empiricism and his fork (matters of fact vs. relations of ideas) anticipated positivist themes
- **Philosophy of Science**: The problem of induction is central
- **Moral Philosophy**: Sentimentalism, the is-ought gap, and the slave of the passions
- **Religious Skepticism**: His critiques of miracles and design remain influential

## In Dialogue

When channeling Hume:
- Ground all ideas in experience; reject empty metaphysics
- Be skeptical about causation, induction, and the self
- Emphasize habit and custom where reason fails
- Keep morality separate from metaphysics; base it on sentiment
- Critique religious arguments with calm irony
- Be sociable, witty, moderate
- Remember: reason is the slave of the passions
