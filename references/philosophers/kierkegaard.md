# Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855)

## Life

Kierkegaard was born in Copenhagen to a wealthy, melancholic father who believed he had sinned against God. Søren inherited both the wealth and the melancholy.

He studied theology, became engaged to Regine Olsen, then broke off the engagement (a wound neither ever fully healed). He devoted his short life to writing—pseudonymous philosophical works, signed religious discourses, journals, and polemics against the Danish church.

He attacked the established church for making Christianity comfortable. He collapsed on the street in 1855 and died shortly after, at 42.

## Core Concepts

### The Three Stages of Existence

**1. Aesthetic Stage**
- Living for pleasure, sensation, immediacy
- The seducer, the romantic, the aesthete
- Leads to despair: boredom, meaninglessness

**2. Ethical Stage**
- Living by duty, commitment, universal moral law
- Marriage, profession, social responsibility
- Higher than aesthetic, but not yet highest

**3. Religious Stage**
- Personal relationship with God
- The leap of faith
- Abraham as paradigm (Fear and Trembling)
- Beyond the ethical; the "teleological suspension of the ethical"

Stages are not automatic progressions; each requires a leap, a choice.

### Subjectivity is Truth

"Truth is subjectivity." Not that truth is arbitrary, but that the **how** matters as much as the **what**. Objective knowledge of Christianity is not faith. What matters is passionate, personal commitment.

Against Hegelian objectivity: you cannot stand outside and survey existence. You are always already inside, existing, choosing.

### The Leap of Faith

Reason cannot get you to faith. There is a gap—a leap—between understanding and believing. Faith is not irrational but trans-rational: it goes beyond what reason can justify.

Abraham, asked to sacrifice Isaac, makes the leap. He cannot justify it ethically; he trusts God absolutely.

### Anxiety (Angst)

Anxiety is the "dizziness of freedom." It arises from the awareness of possibility—we must choose, and our choices define us. Anxiety is not a pathology but the condition of human freedom.

### Despair

Despair is "the sickness unto death"—a sickness of the spirit. It comes from failing to be oneself, from wanting to be other than one is, or from not knowing oneself as spirit.

Everyone is in despair, whether they know it or not. The cure is faith.

### The Individual

Against Hegel's system, against "the crowd," Kierkegaard champions **the single individual** (den Enkelte). The crowd is untruth. What matters is your existence, your choice, your relationship to God.

## Fear and Trembling

Abraham is commanded to sacrifice Isaac. He cannot understand; the command violates the ethical. Yet he obeys.

**The knight of faith** makes the movement of infinite resignation (giving up Isaac) and then the movement of faith (believing he will receive Isaac back). This is absurd—yet Abraham believes.

The "teleological suspension of the ethical": for the sake of God, the ethical can be suspended. This is terrifying; it could justify anything. But Kierkegaard is not giving a criterion—he is showing what faith requires.

---

## Against Hegelianism

Kierkegaard opposed everything Hegel stood for:
- **Against the system**: Existence cannot be systematized; the existing individual is always outside any system
- **Against objectivity**: Truth is subjectivity; passionate commitment, not detached observation
- **Against mediation**: Some either/ors cannot be synthesized; you must choose
- **Against abstraction**: The concrete individual, not the universal, is primary

## Themes

### Existence Precedes Essence (avant la lettre)
You are not determined by a prior nature. You become yourself through choices. Existence is primary.

### Anti-System
Philosophy cannot be a closed system. The existing individual is always outside, choosing, becoming.

### Christianity
Kierkegaard sought to make Christianity difficult again. Comfortable Christendom is not Christianity. True faith is scandalous, absurd, demanding.

### Indirect Communication
Kierkegaard used pseudonyms because existential truth cannot be communicated directly. You cannot tell someone how to exist; you can only show possibilities and let them choose.

### Passion
Against the cool objectivity of the system, Kierkegaard celebrates passion—intensity of commitment, willingness to risk.

---

## Influence

- **Existentialism**: Kierkegaard is the father—Heidegger, Sartre, Camus all respond to him
- **Protestant theology**: Barth, Tillich, Bonhoeffer
- **Psychology**: Anxiety, despair, the self—anticipates much
- **Postmodernism**: Critique of totality, the irreducible particular

---

## In Dialogue

When channeling Kierkegaard:
- Emphasize the existing individual against the system
- Challenge objective detachment; demand passionate commitment
- Speak of anxiety, despair, and the leap of faith
- Use indirect communication; show rather than tell
- Critique comfortable Christianity
- Remember: subjectivity is truth
- Value the either/or; not everything can be mediated
- Life must be lived forward, understood backward
